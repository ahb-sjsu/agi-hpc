# The Lunar Industrial Belt — infrastructure

The Lunar Industrial Belt (LIB) — the near-continuous
band of refineries, mass drivers, solar arrays, mining
operations, and supporting infrastructure that runs
along Luna's equatorial hemisphere — is the single most
important industrial complex in the inner system. The
Belt's visible presence (the arc of lit installations
and reflective surfaces that catches sunlight, visible
from Earth on clear nights) is one of the most familiar
artificial features in human space.

The Belt is *infrastructure*, not a city. The cities
mentioned in their own entries (Tycho, Aristarchus,
Copernicus, Kepler-Reiner) are anchored to the Belt at
specific points; the Belt itself is the connective
tissue and operational base.

## Physical structure

The LIB extends along Luna's equatorial latitude band
(roughly ±15° latitude) for the full circumference of
the moon. The Belt's components include:

- **Mass drivers** — surface-level magnetic-launch
  systems that send bulk cargo (mostly raw materials
  and processed metals) into Lunar orbit. About 12
  major mass drivers operate, with smaller systems
  scattered along the Belt.
- **Refineries** — surface and sub-surface processing
  facilities for Lunar mineral and fusion-fuel
  production
- **Solar arrays** — vast surface installations
  capturing sunlight; approximately 40% of Luna's
  electrical generation. The Belt's equatorial
  position is optimal for solar collection.
- **Mining operations** — surface and sub-surface
  extraction
- **Manufacturing complexes** — heavy-and-light
  industrial production
- **Logistics infrastructure** — the rail, transit,
  and cargo systems that connect the Belt's
  operations
- **Communications relay** — Lunar communications
  infrastructure runs heavily through the Belt

## Operations

The Belt's annual output (rough figures):

- **Helium-3**: ~840 metric tons. Approximately 80% of
  human space's fusion-grade helium-3 originates here.
- **Lithium**: ~12,000 metric tons. ~60% of human
  space's lithium.
- **Titanium-and-aluminum**: substantial fractions of
  inner-system structural materials
- **Rare-earth metals**: ~38% of human space's
  production
- **Manufactured goods (general)**: roughly 22% of
  inner-system manufactured goods by tonnage

The Belt employs approximately 32 million people
directly (across the cities and the smaller industrial
settlements scattered along its length); another 28
million are employed in supporting industries.

## Cities along the Belt

The Belt's anchoring cities (mentioned in their own
entries):

- **Aristarchus** — lithium and rare-earth processing
- **Copernicus** — adjacent to the Belt; not directly
  on it, but economically integrated
- **Kepler-Reiner** — the principal port; freight-
  and-passenger interface
- **Hipparchus** — the Russian-Soviet-legacy city;
  cosmopolitan
- **Mare Imbrium plain settlements** — the densest
  cluster of mid-size cities along the Belt; many
  smaller cities, none individually as large as
  the major nodes

Other cities not on the Belt:

- **Tycho** — south of the Belt; political-cosmopolitan
- **South Pole-Aitken** — water-ice extraction zone;
  separate from the equatorial Belt
- **North Polar** — cold-temperature industry; also
  separate

## The Belt's cultural significance

The Belt is *load-bearing* for the Lunar Republic's
self-image. The Republic was constituted around the
Belt's operations; the political and economic
arguments for Lunar independence in the 2270s-2300s
drew heavily on the Belt's industrial significance.

Cultural markers:

- **Visibility from Earth.** The Belt's bright arc is
  visible from Earth on clear nights. Children on
  Earth learn to identify it; the visual presence is
  one of the few ways the Lunar Republic appears in
  Earth daily life.
- **The mass-driver aesthetic.** The Belt's
  industrial design — long, repetitive, kinetic —
  shapes much of Lunar visual culture. Lunar
  architecture, music, and even literary structure
  reference the mass-driver register.
- **The infrastructural pride.** Lunar identity
  includes substantial pride in *building the
  Belt*. The cultural production engages with this
  — the *Belt Songs*, the literary tradition of
  industrial-historical fiction, the visual-art
  tradition of celebrating workers and engineers.
- **The arcology view.** Most Lunar cities have
  vantage points where the Belt is visible at night;
  the *arc of light* is a common image in Lunar art
  and photography.

## The Belt and Earth-Luna integration

The Belt's economic relationship with Earth is
intimate. Approximately 60% of the Belt's output goes
to Earth; about 80% of Earth's helium-3 (for fusion
power) comes from the Belt. The two economies are
deeply integrated; neither can easily exit the
relationship.

This integration has political consequences. The
Lunar Republic's foreign policy toward Earth has
historically been *cooperative* in part because the
Belt's economic well-being depends on continued
Earth-Luna trade. The Republican Independence Bloc's
arguments for greater autonomy run into the
Belt-economics constraint repeatedly.

## Notes for play

- A Lunar PC's relationship with the Belt is
  *fundamental*. Most Lunar PCs have Belt-related
  family connections, work history, or cultural
  associations.
- The Belt's industrial register is a useful
  texture for engineering and technical PC
  backstories.
- The Belt's visibility from Earth is a useful
  small detail — a Lunar PC may have a specific
  memory of *seeing the Belt from Earth* before
  emigrating, or a memory of *seeing Earth from
  the Belt* during early life.
