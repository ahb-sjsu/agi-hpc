# Luna in 2348 — overview

Luna in 2348 is the workshop of inner human civilization, a
sovereign republic of 220 million people, and the easiest
off-world step for any Earther who wants to leave home
without leaving the gravity well of family. Luna is *close* —
three days by passenger rocket — and *familiar* (its lower
gravity is well-tolerated by adults; its political and
cultural life are openly Earth-derived) and *necessary* (its
industrial base supplies most of human space's bulk
manufactured goods).

## Population and distribution

Luna's 220 million residents are concentrated heavily along
the equatorial **Lunar Industrial Belt** (LIB), a near-
continuous band of cities, refineries, mass drivers, and
solar arrays that stretches across the equatorial
hemisphere. Major cities:

- **Tycho** — 28 million. The political capital of the
  Lunar Republic. South of the equator but counted as
  LIB-adjacent.
- **Aristarchus** — 19 million. The largest pure
  industrial-civilian hybrid city; the lithium and rare-
  earth processing center.
- **Copernicus** — 16 million. Cultural and educational
  center; the University of Luna; the Lunar Symphony.
- **Kepler-Reiner** — 14 million. The merchant-marine
  port; most of Luna's outbound passenger traffic departs
  here.
- **Hipparchus** — 11 million. The older Russian-Soviet
  legacy city, now thoroughly cosmopolitan.
- **Mare Imbrium plain settlements** — 38 million across
  several dozen mid-size cities.

Plus polar settlements:

- **South Pole-Aitken** — 22 million. The principal water-
  ice extraction zone; politically and culturally distinct
  from the equatorial belt.
- **North Polar** — 18 million. Smaller; specialized in
  cold-temperature industry and the Lunar Astronomical
  Reserve.

Plus the orbital infrastructure:

- **The L1 Cluster** — about 4 million people in habitats
  at the Earth-Lunar L1 point. The transit and fueling
  infrastructure that funnels Earth-Luna traffic.
- **The L2 Cluster** — about 2 million. Astronomical
  observatories, deep-space relay stations.
- **The Belt depots** — the orbital staging facilities for
  Luna-Belt traffic.

The remainder (roughly 50 million) is distributed across
smaller settlements, mining operations, agricultural
surface-domes, and the working-class suburbs of the major
cities.

## Government

Luna is the **Lunar Republic**, recognized 2304, governed
under the Constitution of Tycho. Structure:

- **The Lunar Assembly** — unicameral, 280 members,
  apportioned by population, four-year staggered terms.
- **The First Citizen** — ceremonial head of state, six-year
  term, elected by the Assembly. The current First Citizen
  is **Mei Liang**, an industrial physicist by training.
- **The Premier** — head of government, the Assembly's
  majority leader. Currently **Hideo Yamashita**, leading
  a coalition of the Lunar Industrial Party and the
  Republican Independence Bloc.
- **The Lunar Court** — 9 justices, lifetime appointments,
  elected by the Assembly with two-thirds majority.
- **The Industrial Authority** — the agency that regulates
  Luna's heavy industry. Independent of the Assembly's
  political appointments, by constitutional design.
- **The Lunar Defense Service** — Luna's small military.
  Roughly 28,000 personnel; mostly air-defense, customs
  enforcement, and the orbital traffic management. Luna
  does not have a deep-space navy; its security is
  guaranteed by the UNN under the 2304 settlement.

The political culture is more *technocratic* than either
Earth or Mars. Most senior politicians have engineering or
scientific training. Political debates are framed in
quantitative terms (volumes of throughput, energy budgets,
trade flows) more than ideological ones.

The two major parties are:

- **The Lunar Industrial Party** — pro-industry, pro-trade,
  technocratic-centrist. Currently in government.
- **The Republican Independence Bloc** — Lunar-nationalist,
  pro-cultural-autonomy, suspicious of Earth and Mars in
  roughly equal measure. Coalition partner.

A smaller third party, **the Polar Caucus**, represents the
distinct political interests of the South Pole-Aitken and
North Polar regions.

## Economy

Luna's economy is roughly $4.8 trillion UN-equivalent. The
sectoral breakdown:

- **Heavy manufacturing** — 32%. The Lunar Industrial Belt
  produces the bulk of human space's structural metals,
  spacecraft components, mass-driver-launched bulk goods.
- **Resource extraction** — 18%. Helium-3 (for fusion
  reactors), water ice (the main supply for the inner
  system), rare-earths, lithium, the LIB's specialty
  metals.
- **Trade and logistics** — 14%. Luna is the primary inner-
  system trade hub.
- **Tourism** — 8%. Earth's wealthy come to Luna for
  honeymoons, milestone vacations, and the lower-gravity
  recreation industry.
- **Services and finance** — 18%. Comparable to Earth in
  per-capita terms; smaller in absolute scale.
- **Agriculture** — 4%. Surface domes and underground
  hydroponic farms.
- **Defense and government** — 6%.

Luna's per-capita GDP exceeds Earth's by about 35%. The
working population is smaller in fraction than the total
population (Luna has a strong tendency to retain elderly
Earthers as residents — *retire to the Moon* is a real
demographic phenomenon).

## Culture

Lunar culture is *constructed*, not inherited. The rapid
growth of the population (from 1 million in 2200 to 220
million in 2348) means most contemporary Lunar culture has
been deliberately built over the past two centuries.

Notable features:

- **The mass-driver aesthetic.** Lunar industrial design,
  architecture, music, even theater have a recognizable
  *mass-driver* sensibility — long, repetitive, kinetic,
  building toward release. Earthers find the style
  exhausting; Lunars find it natural.
- **The gravity-cultural complex.** Most public spaces on
  Luna are designed for the lower gravity — wider stairs,
  taller ceilings, longer corridors. Visiting Earthers
  feel slightly off; Lunars visiting Earth feel cramped.
- **The polyglot register.** Luna's population is more
  ethnically and linguistically diverse than either Earth
  (homogenizing) or Mars (filtered for self-selection).
  English, Mandarin, Yoruba, Russian, Hindi, Arabic, Bahasa,
  Swahili are all routinely heard in any Lunar city.
- **The Lunar Temple Buddhism.** The major Lunar religious
  innovation. Has spread back to Earth and is the second-
  largest tradition on Luna after Catholic Christianity.
- **The retirement culture.** A substantial fraction of
  Lunar adults are Earth-retirees. The retirement-resort
  district near Aristarchus is its own cultural ecology
  — quieter, more contemplative, with its own restaurants,
  arts venues, and political associations.

## The Earth-Luna relationship

Luna's relationship with Earth is intimate and unequal. Luna's
independence is real but qualified: the UN's L1 station has
veto authority over Lunar orbital approaches, the UNN's
Inner-System Command operates in cislunar space without
Lunar permission, and Luna's defense umbrella is provided by
Earth.

The political consequence: Luna is genuinely independent on
all internal matters and constrained on external matters.
Most Lunars accept this as a reasonable trade. A vocal
minority (the Republican Independence Bloc, parts of the
Polar Caucus) wants more autonomy. The current settlement is
stable but not eternal.

The economic consequence: Earth and Luna are deeply
integrated trade partners. About 60% of Luna's manufactured
goods go to Earth. About 80% of the helium-3 that fuels
Earth's fusion power plants comes from Luna. The integration
is mutual; neither side can easily exit.

## The Mars-Luna relationship

Luna and Mars have a more distant relationship — partly
because they are in different parts of the inner system,
partly because the Lunar economy is Earth-oriented, and
partly because the Lunar political class regards Mars's
republican fervor as melodramatic.

Trade is moderate, cultural exchange is limited, and
political coordination is rare. The two governments meet
formally about three times a year. Most Lunars regard Mars
with friendly distance; most Martians regard Luna with mild
condescension (*you call yourselves a republic? you have a
First Citizen.*).

## The Belt-Luna relationship

The Belt and Luna have a working economic relationship.
Belters supply Luna with rarer ores and specialty manufactured
goods; Luna supplies the Belt with the heavy industrial
output the Belt's smaller industrial base cannot produce.

Politically, Luna's posture toward the OPA is correct,
distant, and slightly ambivalent. Lunar conservatives think
of the Belt as messy. Lunar progressives think of the Belt
as exploited and admirable. Lunar pragmatists trade with
whoever pays.

## Notes for play

- A Luna-born PC has a very different *frame* from an
  Earth or Mars PC. Lunars grew up in lower gravity, in a
  multilingual setting, in a constructed culture, and
  with an explicit awareness of being *young* as a polity.
  They take less for granted than Earthers and less
  seriously than Martians.
- Luna is the *easy* off-world step in any Earther
  backstory. A character whose *only* off-world experience
  is a Luna trip is the most common shape.
- The retirement culture is a useful narrative resource
  for older NPCs — the Earth-born retiree-on-Luna who
  knows things from the previous century.
- Luna is not central to the campaign. The *Halyard*
  doesn't visit Luna. But many PCs will pass through
  Luna on the way to Ceres, and Lunar memories may
  surface during the long transit.
