# The Belt in 2348 — overview

The Belt — the asteroid Belt, the Jovian moons, the Saturnian
moons, the trailing-Trojan habitats, and the dispersed
Kuiper-edge stations — is the third pillar of human
civilization, the one most distant from Earth and Mars in
both physical and cultural distance. The Belt is where the
people who didn't fit on Earth or Mars went, and where their
grandchildren now live as the inheritors of a culture that
has grown its own physical morphology, its own language, and
its own politics.

The Belt is not a state. It is a sprawling set of stations,
ships, and small worlds connected by trade, kinship,
linguistic affinity, and the irregular institutions of the
Outer Planets Alliance. There is no Belter government in the
sense Earth or Mars would recognize. There is, however, a
Belter *polity* — a network of overlapping authorities,
guilds, federations, and informal accords that, on most
practical questions, behaves like one.

## Population

Roughly **480 million Belters** are distributed across
human space outside Earth-Luna and Mars-Phobos-Deimos.

The major concentrations:

- **Ceres** — 28 million. The largest single Belt city.
- **Vesta** — 11 million. The mining capital.
- **Pallas** — 8 million.
- **Hygiea** — 5 million.
- **The Trojan habitats** — 14 million across roughly 80
  cluster habitats at the Sun-Jupiter L4 and L5 points.
- **Ganymede** — 18 million. The largest Jovian-system
  population. Joint OPA-MCR-UN administered, in practice
  Belter culturally.
- **Callisto** — 9 million. Mining and refining.
- **Europa** — 4 million. The smallest Jovian system body
  but the most politically prominent (the **Europa
  Treaty Authority** has a complicated relationship with
  the OPA).
- **Io** — 2 million. Specialized in volcanic-cycle
  industries.
- **Titan** — 2.4 million. Saturn's largest moon and the
  Saturnian system's center.
- **Enceladus and the inner Saturnian moons** — 600 thousand.
- **The dispersed Kuiper-edge stations** — about 1.2
  million in roughly 240 stations between 30 and 60 AU.

Plus the *spinners* — the population on permanently-spinning
ships that don't dock at any single station for long. About
3.5 million Belters live this way; the spinner population is
the most distinctive Belt subculture.

## Language

The Belt's lingua franca is **Lang Belta** — the creole that
emerged from the labor migrations of the early settlement
period. Lang Belta has roots in English, Mandarin, Yoruba,
Hindi, Russian, Bahasa, Spanish, and Swahili, with
contributions from at least a dozen other Earth languages
and the development of distinctive Belt-specific
vocabulary.

Most Belters speak Lang Belta and at least one Earth
language fluently. Educated Belters often speak two or
three. Lang Belta is the working language of the OPA, of
most Belt commercial transactions, and of the Belt's
internal cultural production.

Lang Belta has produced an extensive Belt literary
tradition. The poet **Vasilios Okeke** (Ceres, 2298–2371)
is widely considered the language's greatest writer.

## Physical morphology

Belters who grew up in low gravity have distinctive physical
adaptations:

- **Taller average stature** (median 195 cm for adult men,
  185 cm for adult women).
- **Longer limbs**, particularly distinctive elongated
  fingers.
- **Larger heads** relative to body size.
- **Reduced bone density** that requires medical management
  for any extended trip into Earth-normal gravity.
- **Distinct facial structure** — broader brows, wider
  eyes, a particular nasal-bridge shape.

These traits are partly developmental (low-gravity
upbringing) and partly genetic (200 years of selection
pressure for traits that work in space).

A Belter visiting Earth requires medical preparation and
gravity-adaptation drugs; even with these, prolonged Earth
visits are dangerous. Belters in their fifties and sixties
who visit Earth without preparation can die from cardiac
events. The Earth-as-graveyard reputation is a real Belter
cultural marker.

## OPA

The **Outer Planets Alliance** is the closest thing the Belt
has to a central political organization. It is not a state.
It is a federation of:

- **Labor unions** (the asteroid miners, the spinner crews,
  the station workers' associations)
- **Trade guilds** (the merchant marines, the engineers'
  syndicates)
- **Political organizations** (provincial associations on
  the major bodies, advocacy groups, cultural federations)
- **A few outright militias** (the Beratnas Gang, the
  Brennt Coalition, smaller groups; mostly historical
  relics with diminished current presence)

The OPA's central body is the **Plenum**, which meets
quarterly at rotating sites and is composed of delegates
from member organizations. The Plenum can pass resolutions
but has no enforcement authority. Its primary function is
*coordination* — the alignment of member organizations'
positions on shared issues.

The OPA's political effectiveness is therefore variable. On
issues of broad Belt consensus (the Earth-Belt customs
regime, treatment of Belter bodies in Inner-System
hospitals, recognition of Lang Belta in international
proceedings) the OPA acts coherently. On issues of
intra-Belt disagreement (the relationship with Mars, the
Europa question, the Kuiper-edge governance debate) the
OPA fragments.

The OPA's *external face* — the diplomats and political
negotiators who represent the Belt to Earth, Mars, and
Luna — is more coherent than its internal life suggests.
The current OPA Spokeswoman is **Naomi Daniyal**, a
veteran Ceres-based politician who has held the role since
2342 and is widely respected on all sides.

## Government, in practice

How is the Belt actually governed, in the absence of a
state? Through layered authorities:

1. **Local station authority.** Each station has its own
   council, port authority, and (usually) corporate
   sponsor. Local laws are made and enforced locally.
2. **Provincial authority.** Major bodies (Ceres, Vesta,
   Pallas, Ganymede, Titan) have provincial governments
   with broader jurisdiction.
3. **Treaty regimes.** Specific issues are handled by
   specific treaties — the Europa Treaty Authority for
   Europa's protected biosphere, the Jovian Customs
   Compact for Jupiter-system trade, the Trans-Neptunian
   Information Compact for Kuiper-edge data sharing.
4. **OPA coordination.** For Belt-wide issues.
5. **Bilateral or multilateral diplomacy with Earth, Mars,
   and Luna** for inter-civilization issues.

The system is messy, redundant, and prone to gaps. It has
also worked, in its current form, since the 2280s. Most
Belters are skeptical of more centralized alternatives.

## Economy

The Belt economy is hard to measure (no central
statistical authority, distributed governance, complex
informal sector) but is estimated at $11–13 trillion
UN-equivalent.

Sectoral structure (very rough):

- **Mining and resource extraction** — 28%. The Belt's
  defining sector.
- **Manufacturing** — 18%. Specialized; spacecraft, fusion
  components, the high-end machine tools that Earth's
  larger industry doesn't quite replicate.
- **Transportation and logistics** — 16%. The merchant
  marines, the spinners, the station-to-station shipping.
- **Services** — 14%. Mostly station-internal.
- **Trade** — 10%.
- **Agriculture** — 6%. Hydroponic, mostly Belt-internal.
- **Defense and security** — 4%. Mostly OPA-coordinated.
- **Other** — 4%.

Per-capita GDP is roughly 65% of Earth's. Cost of living
is highly variable: low on the major bodies, very high on
the smaller stations and the spinners.

## The Belt's relationship with the rest of the system

The Belt's external relationships are complicated:

- **With Earth** — Adversarial-correct. The Belt's labor
  feeds Earth's manufacturing supply chain; Earth's
  customs regimes constrain Belt commercial activity. The
  relationship is functional but rarely warm. Many Belters
  hold a grievance against Earth that is generations
  deep.
- **With Mars** — Complicated. Mars and the Belt cooperated
  on Mars's secession; the post-secession MCR has, in
  Belter eyes, not adequately reciprocated. The
  relationship has been improving since the 2336 crisis
  but remains slightly cool.
- **With Luna** — Pragmatic. Luna and the Belt trade
  heavily; both governments find the relationship
  uncomplicated.
- **Internal Belt politics** — fragmented. The Jovians and
  Saturnians have their own subcultures and partial
  separatist tendencies. The Kuiper-edge populations are
  too dispersed to have a unified politics.

## The *Halyard* contract context

Mao-Kwikowski Mercantile is a Belt-flagged consortium with
deep OPA-adjacency (not formally OPA-aligned, but with
relationships in the Plenum). The *Halyard* is registered
on Ceres. Captain Marsh's commercial reputation in the
Belt is substantial — she has run Belt-flagged contracts
for fifteen years.

The Belt's posture toward the *Halyard* contract is
*interested but not intervening*. The OPA's Outer
Coordination Office has a file on the contract; it is not
the largest file in the office. The contract's eventual
result — whatever the *Halyard* finds at Nithon — will be
of intense interest to multiple Belt-internal factions.

## Notes for play

- A Belter PC has the most distinct cultural signature of
  any of the major options. Lean into Lang Belta phrasing
  in dialogue, the physical morphology, the suspicion of
  Earth, the affection for the spinner life.
- The OPA is not a monolith. A Belter PC's specific
  faction loyalty (which provincial association? which
  union? which cultural federation?) shapes their
  political instincts.
- Belters in deep-range crews are common. Most spinners
  rotate through deep-range work as a matter of course.
- The Belt is *not* the campaign's antagonist. The Belt
  is, in some sense, the campaign's *home* — the *Halyard*
  is Belt-flagged, Belt-registered, and Belt-bound.
