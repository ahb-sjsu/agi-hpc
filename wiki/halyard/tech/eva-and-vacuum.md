# EVA and vacuum survival

EVA (Extra-Vehicular Activity) operations and
vacuum survival are the *operational core* of
deep-range work in 2348. The technology, training,
and protocols around EVA have matured substantially
over the past century; the *Halyard*'s crew will
spend significant time in EVA conditions during
the Nithon surface phase, and most of them have
extensive prior EVA experience.

This entry covers operational protocols and
equipment beyond what's covered in
`tech/civilian-armor.md` (which addresses suit
hardware itself).

## Vacuum survival principles

The fundamental physics of vacuum exposure has
not changed. Specifics:

### Immediate effects

- **Pressure differential** — the lungs and
  ear-drums experience immediate pressure
  differential when exposed to vacuum
- **Boiling of body fluids** — exposed body
  fluids (saliva, tears, sweat) boil
  immediately
- **Blood-oxygen depletion** — the
  pulmonary system stops oxygenating; body-
  oxygen reserves last roughly 90 seconds
- **Cold** — substantial radiative cooling
  in vacuum
- **Radiation** — substantial direct exposure
  to solar radiation (UV, X-ray, particle
  flux)

### Survival window

- **First 15 seconds** — vacuum exposure does
  not produce immediate death; survival is
  possible if rescued within this window
- **15-90 seconds** — substantial damage but
  often recoverable
- **90-180 seconds** — major injury;
  recovery uncertain
- **Beyond 180 seconds** — typically fatal;
  recovery rare even with substantial
  intervention

The 90-180 second window is the operational
*decision space*. Most aboard-ship vacuum
emergencies are resolved within this window or
not at all.

### Common myths

- **Vacuum does not cause explosive
  decompression of the body**. The skin and
  tissue are adequately resilient to maintain
  body integrity for short periods.
- **Vacuum does not cause immediate freezing**.
  Radiative cooling is substantial but slow;
  the immediate cold-injury risk is less than
  twenty-first-century fiction suggested.

## EVA training

Standard EVA training for outer-system
contractors includes:

### Basic training

- **Suit donning and doffing** — efficiency
  and reliability under stress
- **Suit operations** — life-support
  management, sensor interpretation,
  communication
- **Movement in vacuum** — both 0-g (free
  movement) and 0.x-g (low-gravity surface)
- **Tool-using in EVA** — operating tools
  while wearing EVA suits
- **Emergency protocols** — buddy-checks,
  emergency-egress, suit-failure response

### Advanced training

- **Long-duration EVA** — for shifts of
  several hours
- **Hazardous-environment EVA** — for
  specific hazardous-environment work
- **EVA work in radiation environments** —
  for high-radiation environments
- **Combat EVA** — for tactical EVA
  operations (relevant for security
  personnel)

### Certification levels

- **Standard EVA-certified** — basic
  EVA capability; required for any
  outer-system contract work
- **Advanced EVA-certified** — substantial
  EVA capability; required for specialty
  applications
- **Combat-EVA-certified** — substantial
  tactical EVA capability; restricted to
  specific roles

The *Halyard*'s crew certification:

- **All crew** — Standard EVA-certified
- **Surface Operations Specialist** — Advanced
  EVA-certified
- **Engineering crew** — Advanced EVA-certified
- **Cross** — Combat-EVA-certified
- **Halverson** — Standard EVA-certified

## Aboard-ship vacuum protocols

### Routine

- **Pressure monitoring** — continuous;
  alarms for any pressure-loss indication
- **Bulkhead integrity** — continuous
  monitoring; automatic sealing of
  compartments showing pressure-loss
- **EVA suit ready-availability** — most
  ship's compartments have at least one
  emergency EVA suit available
- **Crew training on emergency procedures**
  — refresher drills monthly

### Emergency response

- **Pressure-loss alarms** — the ship's
  systems sound alarms immediately on any
  significant pressure loss
- **Automatic sealing** — affected
  compartments are automatically sealed
  by the ship's systems
- **Crew response** — trained protocols
  for individual response to vacuum
  emergencies
- **Medical response** — substantial
  capability for treating decompression
  injury

## Surface EVA protocols

For surface operations on bodies like Nithon:

### Pre-EVA

- **Suit checks** — comprehensive checks
  before any EVA
- **Atmospheric assessment** — for the
  surface environment
- **Mission planning** — for the specific
  EVA objectives
- **Emergency planning** — for various
  failure modes

### During EVA

- **Buddy system** — most surface EVAs are
  conducted with at least 2 personnel
- **Continuous communications** — with
  the ship and with EVA partners
- **Resource monitoring** — oxygen,
  power, water; staying within established
  margins
- **Environmental awareness** — temperature,
  radiation, hazards

### Post-EVA

- **Decontamination** — for specific
  hazardous-environment applications
- **Equipment maintenance** — substantial
  post-EVA maintenance is standard
- **Health monitoring** — for radiation
  exposure, decompression effects, etc.

## Vacuum-related campaign scenarios

### Routine

- **Outbound burn EVA** — minor maintenance
  during the long outbound burn
- **Cryo-block EVA** — limited; mostly
  sealed-environment work
- **Approach EVA** — preparation for the
  Nithon surface phase

### Surface phase

- **Initial surface assessment** — first
  surface EVA on Nithon
- **Sustained surface operations** — extended
  surface work over days to weeks
- **Specialty surface operations** — gate-
  related operations if the campaign reaches
  those beats

### Emergency

- **Pressure-loss emergencies** — possibilities
  during the long transit
- **Suit-failure emergencies** — during EVA
  operations
- **Hazardous-environment emergencies** —
  during surface operations

## Notes for play

- **EVA is dangerous**. The campaign treats
  it seriously; PCs should treat it
  seriously.
- **The *Halyard*'s EVA capability is good**
  but not unlimited. PCs should plan
  carefully for surface operations.
- **Trained-personnel-only operations** —
  some EVA operations require advanced or
  combat certification. PCs without those
  certifications should not undertake those
  operations.
- **Buddy-system protocols** are standard.
  PCs who undertake solo EVA operations are
  taking on substantial additional risk.
