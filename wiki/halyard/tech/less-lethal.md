# Less-lethal weapons

Less-lethal weapons in 2348 occupy an operationally
important niche: they are the *aboard-ship-safe*
options that can be used in pressurized environments
without the catastrophic risks of conventional
firearms. Most spacecraft, including the *Halyard*,
carry significant less-lethal weapon inventories
specifically because lethal weapons are
operationally inappropriate.

The categories: chemical (gas), electrical (stun),
sonic, electromagnetic, and kinetic less-lethal.
Each has specific applications and limitations.

## Chemical (gas) weapons

### Tear gas and irritant gases

Standard non-toxic irritants:

- **CS-equivalent compounds** — produces severe
  eye-and-respiratory irritation; recovery
  typically minutes to hours
- **OC (oleoresin capsicum)-equivalent** — pepper-
  spray-class irritants; hand-deployed and
  weapon-deployed variants
- **Sleep gases (anesthetic)** — produce loss of
  consciousness; require specific dosing for
  safety; veterinary and military origins

Aboard-ship limitations: gas weapons can be used
aboard ship under specific conditions (filtration
systems must be operational; affected personnel
must be evacuable; the gas must be containable in
the immediate area). Major gas deployment aboard
ship is rare.

### Specialty chemical agents

Beyond the basic irritants:

- **Vomiting agents** — for crowd-control in
  uncontained environments
- **Sleep agents** — for incapacitation; some
  formulations are sufficiently safe for crowd
  use, others require careful dosing
- **Disorientation agents** — produce confusion
  without unconsciousness; recovery typically
  hours

## Electrical (stun) weapons

### Electroshock weapons

Modern electroshock devices:

- **Stun batons** — handheld; contact-required;
  produces immediate temporary incapacitation
  and substantial pain
- **Electroshock guns** — projectile; deploys
  conductive darts at range; produces immediate
  temporary incapacitation
- **Wave-stun systems** — area-effect; produces
  temporary incapacitation across a designated
  area

Most modern electroshock weapons are *electronically
controlled*: they can be set to specific
incapacitation levels, deliver controlled-duration
pulses, and avoid the cardiac-event risks that
twentieth-century equivalents had.

Electroshock weapons are *aboard-ship-safe* — they
do not produce pressure-loss risks, gas-system risks,
or major collateral damage.

### Stun rifles

Higher-power versions of stun guns; longer effective
range; substantial incapacitation capability. Used
by some military and law-enforcement forces.

## Sonic weapons

### Acoustic disorientation devices

- **Sonic guns / acoustic weapons** — produce
  high-intensity sound at frequencies that produce
  disorientation, nausea, or pain
- **Long-range acoustic devices** — area-effect;
  capable of communicating-or-incapacitating at
  substantial range
- **Specialty applications** — including
  underwater sonic weapons (for protection of
  Pacific platform habitats and similar)

Aboard-ship safety: sonic weapons are *generally*
aboard-ship-safe but produce hearing-damage risks at
high intensity. Most aboard-ship-safe protocols
require ear protection for users.

### Subsonic-disturbance devices

Less common; produce disorientation through
infrasonic frequencies. Specialized applications.

## Electromagnetic weapons

### Electromagnetic pulse devices

- **Localized EMP** — disable electronic devices
  in a specific area
- **EMP grenades** — for tactical use; small-area
  effect
- **Larger EMP weapons** — for installation
  defense; substantial area-effect

EMP weapons are *concerning* aboard-ship — many
shipboard systems are EMP-hardened, but the risks
are substantial. Aboard-ship use is heavily
restricted.

### Targeted electronic disruption

More precise versions of EMP that target specific
electronic systems without producing area-effect
disruption. Used primarily by intelligence and
specialty military forces.

## Kinetic less-lethal

### Beanbag and rubber-bullet munitions

Conventional firearms loaded with non-lethal
projectiles:

- **Beanbag rounds** — large fabric-wrapped
  projectiles; produce blunt-force trauma without
  penetration
- **Rubber bullets** — similar; smaller projectiles
- **Specialty kinetic rounds** — including
  specifically-designed non-lethal projectiles
  for various scenarios

These are typically deployed from standard shotgun
or specialty launcher platforms.

### Net launchers

Mechanically-deployed nets for restraint:

- **Hand-thrown nets** — small-area; close-range
- **Rifle-launched nets** — larger; medium-range
- **Vehicle-mounted nets** — for vehicle
  restraint applications

## Aboard the *Halyard*

The *Halyard*'s less-lethal inventory:

- **2 less-lethal launchers** — sonic and
  electroshock variants
- **Stun batons** — issued to security personnel
- **Sleep-gas grenades** — limited inventory;
  emergency use only
- **Restraint nets** — hand-deployed; for
  immediate use

The launchers are stored in the arms locker. The
batons may be carried by security personnel
(specifically Cross). The sleep-gas grenades are
under captain's lock; deployment requires explicit
authorization.

## Operational doctrine aboard ship

The *Halyard*'s standing rules of engagement
emphasize less-lethal escalation:

1. **Verbal de-escalation** — first response
2. **Restraint hardware** — for immediate
   physical incapacitation
3. **Less-lethal weapons** — for situations
   requiring active intervention
4. **Lethal weapons** — only with captain's
   explicit authorization, and only when
   less-lethal options are exhausted

This doctrine is standard for civilian deep-range
vessels. It reflects both the legal-regulatory
framework and the practical realities of aboard-
ship combat.

## Notes for play

- **Less-lethal options exist for most
  shipboard situations.** A PC who needs to
  incapacitate someone aboard ship has multiple
  non-lethal tools available.
- **The *less-lethal* nomenclature is honest.**
  These weapons can produce severe injury and
  occasional fatality, particularly with
  pre-existing conditions or specific
  vulnerabilities. Players should not treat them
  as risk-free.
- **Aboard-ship combat using less-lethal is
  realistic** for the campaign's likely
  scenarios. Most aboard-ship conflict will be
  resolved with less-lethal force rather than
  firearms.
