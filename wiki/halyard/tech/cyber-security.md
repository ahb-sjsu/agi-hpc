# Cyber-security in 2348

Cyber-security in 2348 is a *mature* discipline.
Two centuries of refinement after the early
twenty-first-century cyber-security crises have
produced an environment in which:

- **Most consumer-grade systems** are reasonably
  secure against ordinary attacks
- **Professional systems** are substantially
  secure against most serious attacks
- **Intelligence-class operations** can still
  penetrate most systems with sufficient
  resources
- **The defensive-offensive balance** remains
  roughly even — every defense has corresponding
  attacks; every attack has corresponding defenses

The campaign's underlying assumption: cyber-security
is *real* and *substantial*; PCs cannot expect to
hack arbitrary systems for plot convenience; but
specific operations remain possible with appropriate
resources and skill.

## The threat landscape

### Attack categories

- **Network attacks** — substantially mitigated by
  encryption and segmentation; specialized attacks
  remain effective against specific configurations
- **Endpoint attacks** — direct compromise of
  devices; the most common attack category in
  contemporary operations
- **Supply-chain attacks** — compromising
  components or software during manufacture
- **Social engineering** — manipulation of humans
  to bypass technical controls; remains the most
  effective attack against well-defended targets
- **Side-channel attacks** — extracting
  information from electromagnetic, thermal, or
  other side-channels; technically demanding but
  effective against specific targets
- **Physical attacks** — device theft, physical
  network access, electromagnetic disruption

### Threat actors

- **Nation-state intelligence** (FSec, MIB, others)
  — the most capable category; substantial
  resources; full-spectrum capability
- **Major corporate intelligence** (Protogen, Lloyd's,
  others) — substantial; particularly capable in
  specific domains
- **Organized crime** — moderate; mostly profit-
  motivated; operates outside formal legal
  frameworks
- **Hacktivist groups** — variable; substantial
  capability in specific domains
- **Lone-wolf actors** — limited but occasionally
  consequential

## Defensive technology

### Encryption

The foundation of contemporary cyber-security:

- **Symmetric encryption** — multiple mature
  ciphers; quantum-resistant choices well-understood
- **Asymmetric encryption** — post-quantum
  schemes are mature and dominant; the
  pre-quantum schemes of 2025 are *deprecated*
  for sensitive applications
- **Key exchange** — quantum-key distribution
  for high-security applications; classical
  post-quantum schemes for ordinary use
- **Forward secrecy** — universal in modern
  protocols
- **Authenticated encryption** — universal

### Access control

- **Multi-factor authentication** — universal;
  most systems require multiple factors
- **Biometric authentication** — substantial;
  most consumer devices use biometric primary
  authentication
- **Cryptographic identity** — substantial;
  hardware-backed cryptographic credentials
- **Zero-trust architectures** — dominant for
  professional applications

### Network defense

- **Encrypted channels** — universal
- **Segmented networks** — substantial; major
  systems are heavily compartmentalized
- **Anomaly detection** — substantial; AI-based
  monitoring across network traffic
- **Intrusion-detection** — universal in
  professional systems

### Endpoint security

- **Trusted-platform-module** equivalents —
  universal; hardware-backed security across
  most consumer and professional devices
- **Secure-execution environments** — universal
  for sensitive operations
- **Mandatory access control** — dominant in
  professional systems

## Vulnerabilities

Despite the substantial defensive maturity, real
vulnerabilities exist:

### Endpoint compromise

- **Targeted exploitation** of specific endpoint
  vulnerabilities
- **Supply-chain compromise** of devices or
  software before deployment
- **Physical compromise** of devices
- **Insider threat** — authorized users abusing
  their access

### Cryptographic vulnerabilities

- **Implementation flaws** — even strong
  cryptographic algorithms can be vulnerable
  to implementation errors
- **Key compromise** — through endpoint
  compromise, social engineering, or physical
  access
- **Side-channel leakage** — through power-
  consumption, electromagnetic, or timing
  channels

### Specific high-value targets

The campaign's relevant intelligence services have
substantial cyber capability:

- **FSec** — full-spectrum; substantial offensive
  capability against UNN-jurisdiction targets
  and (with operational discipline) against
  external targets
- **MIB** — full-spectrum; particularly capable
  against UN-jurisdiction targets
- **Lloyd's intelligence** — substantial
  capability in financial and underwriting-
  related operations
- **OPA security** — variable; substantial in
  some specialty domains

## Aboard-spacecraft cyber-security

The *Halyard*'s cyber-security profile:

### Standard infrastructure

- **Encrypted internal network** — all internal
  communications encrypted
- **Multi-factor crew authentication** — for
  access to sensitive systems
- **Segmented systems** — life-support, propulsion,
  navigation, and communications are on separate
  network segments
- **Logging** — comprehensive; aboard-ship
  activity is logged for audit purposes
- **External-network firewalling** — substantial;
  most external communications go through
  controlled gateways

### SIGMA-4's role

The ship-mind has substantial responsibility for
cyber-security:

- Continuous monitoring of internal and external
  network activity
- Anomaly detection across system behaviors
- Authentication services for crew access to
  sensitive systems
- Audit-log management

SIGMA-4 herself is not a *hacking target* in any
ordinary sense — her access is authorized and
substantial. Compromise of SIGMA-4 would require
substantial operational resources.

### Vulnerabilities

The *Halyard* is reasonably secure against ordinary
attacks. It is *not* secure against:

- **Substantial nation-state attack** — FSec, MIB,
  or equivalent could compromise the ship if they
  prioritized it
- **Insider threat** — a crew member with
  substantial system access (Halverson, the
  ship's master, the chief engineer) could
  conduct substantial operations against the
  ship's systems if motivated
- **Physical compromise** — the ship's systems
  are accessible to anyone aboard with
  appropriate access

## Operational implications for play

### Communications privacy

- **Crew-to-Crew communications** aboard ship
  are *moderately* private — encrypted, but
  visible to SIGMA-4 and to authorized auditors
- **Crew-to-external communications** are
  visible to multiple intelligence services
  through metadata
- **Sensitive conversations** require *physical
  isolation* — face-to-face in private spaces
  with confirmed counter-surveillance

### Computer-system access

- **Routine access** — open to all crew with
  appropriate clearances
- **Privileged access** — limited to specific
  crew (Captain, Chief Engineer, Security Officer)
- **Restricted access** — even more limited;
  specific systems for specific crew

### Hacking and intrusion

- **Player-driven hacking attempts** should
  generally be possible only with substantial
  Cyber-skill checks, time investment, and
  appropriate equipment
- **NPC-driven hacking attempts** may be the
  source of plot complications; the campaign's
  intelligence-themes include cyber-operations
  among the available techniques
- **Halverson's potential cyber-operations**
  through ARTEMIS are a Keeper-relevant
  consideration. ARTEMIS has substantial
  computational capability that could, in
  principle, be used for cyber-operations the
  rest of the crew is not aware of.

## Notes for play

- **Cyber-security is *real*.** Players should
  not expect to hack arbitrary systems casually;
  serious cyber-operations require substantial
  preparation and skill.
- **The *Halyard*'s systems are well-defended**
  against ordinary attacks but vulnerable to
  determined nation-state attack and to insider
  operations.
- **SIGMA-4 is a defensive asset** but is not
  a complete cyber-security solution. The ship
  remains vulnerable in specific scenarios.
- **The campaign's intelligence themes**
  include cyber-operations as one mechanism.
  Plot reveals through cyber-investigation are
  a useful Keeper resource.
