# Military firearms in 2348

![Standard service rifles compared — M-340, RR-12, L-2 light](https://halyard.atlas-sjsu.duckdns.org/art/service-rifles.svg)

![M-340 service rifle schematic](https://halyard.atlas-sjsu.duckdns.org/art/weapons/m-340.svg)

Military firearms in 2348 fall into three broad
categories: *small arms* (carried by individual
combatants), *crew-served weapons* (heavy weapons
typically requiring multiple operators), and
*ship-mounted weapons* (the largest naval-scale
weapons). The technology has matured along the
trajectory 2025 anticipated; recognizable continuity
with twenty-first-century weapons exists, with
substantial improvements in efficiency, reliability,
and integration.

## Small arms

### Standard service rifles

The principal infantry weapons of the UNN, MCRN, and
related forces:

- **The UNN service rifle (M-340)** — gas-operated
  semi-automatic and three-round-burst rifle; 5.56mm-
  equivalent caliber; smart-sight integration;
  multiple ammunition types (standard, frangible,
  specialty). Standard issue across UNN services.
- **The MCRN service rifle (RR-12)** — similar
  performance to the UNN's M-340 but with
  manufacturer differences (Tharsis-built); slightly
  better performance at the cost of greater weight.
  Standard MCRN issue.
- **The Lunar Defense Service rifle** — typically
  uses UNN-derived designs under Lunar
  manufacturing. Smaller forces; less standardization.
- **OPA security rifles** — variable. Most stations
  use commercial-grade weapons rather than
  military-grade. Some specialty OPA security
  forces use weapons comparable to UNN/MCRN service
  rifles.

### Sidearms

Standard sidearms are 9mm or .40-equivalent
semi-automatic handguns with smart-sight integration.
The UNN service sidearm is the **M-310**; the MCRN's
is the **PR-08**. Most small forces use commercial
designs.

### Battle rifles and specialty weapons

- **The UNN sniper rifle (M-340-S)** — bolt-action
  or specialty semi-automatic; .338-equivalent or
  larger calibers; substantial smart-sight and
  ballistic-computation integration
- **The MCRN sniper rifle (RR-12-S)** — equivalent
  capability; Mars-manufactured
- **Specialty designs** — for specific operational
  requirements; usually small-volume production

## Crew-served weapons

### Heavy machine guns

The standard crew-served small-arms-class weapon. Used
in fixed installations and on vehicles:

- **UNN M-580** — .50-equivalent; standard issue
- **MCRN R-840** — equivalent capability
- Rate of fire 600-800 rounds per minute typical
- Sustained-fire capability with cooling systems

### Anti-armor weapons

- **Light anti-armor rocket launchers** — shoulder-
  fired; effective against light vehicles
- **Medium anti-armor missiles** — crew-served;
  effective against most armored vehicles
- **Heavy anti-armor systems** — vehicle-mounted;
  effective against most armored targets

### Mortars

- **Light mortars** — squad-level; 60mm-equivalent
- **Medium mortars** — battalion-level; 81mm-
  equivalent
- **Heavy mortars** — battery-level; 120mm-
  equivalent

### Anti-aircraft / air-defense

- **Man-portable air-defense missiles** — for
  vehicle and small-unit defense
- **Vehicle-mounted air-defense systems** — for
  unit defense
- **Theater-level air-defense** — for major
  installations

## Ship-mounted weapons

The defining feature of naval combat in 2348. The
major categories:

### Point Defense Cannons (PDCs)

The most numerous ship-mounted weapon. PDCs are:

- High-rate-of-fire automatic cannons (typically
  20-30mm; some larger)
- Computer-controlled with substantial automation
- Used primarily for defense against incoming
  missiles and small craft
- Universally mounted on naval and many merchant
  vessels

The *Halyard* has a single decommissioned PDC mount
(aft); the gun has been removed but the mount
remains. Restoration is mechanically possible but
would require regulatory approval the consortium has
not pursued.

PDCs use specialized ammunition. The *Modified PDC
Rounds* mentioned in House Rules §7.2 — ammunition
selected to be aboard-ship-safe (frangible, limited-
penetration, controlled-overpenetration) — are
standard for the small-arms-class PDCs that are
sometimes carried as personal-or-crew-served weapons.

### Naval railguns

Magnetic-launch projectile weapons:

- High-velocity (multi-kilometers-per-second
  projectile)
- Long-range (effective at hundreds to thousands of
  kilometers in space)
- Substantial energy requirements
- Mounted on capital ships and large warships

The MCRN's Donnager-class capital ships and the UNN's
Tsavo-class carriers carry railguns.

### Missiles

Ship-launched missiles in 2348 are:

- **Anti-ship missiles** — long-range; substantial
  warheads; capable of penetrating most ship
  defenses with substantial salvos
- **Anti-fighter missiles** — short-range; smaller
  warheads; primarily defensive
- **Anti-station missiles** — large-warhead;
  long-range; specifically designed for
  installation targets

Missile families have been substantially refined
since 2025; performance is correspondingly better.
The MCRN and UNN both maintain substantial missile
inventories.

### Energy weapons

- **High-energy lasers** — limited deployment.
  Used primarily for defense against missiles and
  small craft. Energy weapons in 2348 face the
  challenge that they require enormous power
  during use; sustainable laser fire requires
  major reactor capacity.
- **Particle beam weapons** — research and
  prototype; not deployed at scale

## Naval doctrine

The naval-doctrine implications of these weapons:

- **PDCs are universal** for ship defense
- **Railguns and missiles** are the principal
  offensive systems
- **Capital-ship engagements** are *expensive* —
  major ships consume substantial missile
  inventory and energy budgets
- **Most naval action is defensive** — coercive
  diplomacy rather than full force-on-force
  combat

## Aboard the *Halyard*

The *Halyard*'s naval-weapon profile is *minimal*:

- **The decommissioned aft PDC mount** — gun
  removed; mount remains. Mechanically possible
  to restore; regulatorily complicated.
- **No active offensive weapons.**
- **No missile capability.**
- **No naval-scale energy weapons.**

This is *typical* for civilian deep-range vessels.
The *Halyard* is a science vessel, not a warship.

## Notes for play

- **The Halyard cannot fight back** against a
  serious adversary in any meaningful way. The
  campaign's design assumes this.
- **The presence of military forces** (UNN Outer
  Authority cruisers, MCRN Third Fleet vessels)
  is a *background* presence rather than an
  immediate threat.
- **Cross's training** on naval-scale weapons is
  substantial. He can advise on what's
  happening if the *Halyard* is in proximity to
  naval forces. He cannot, however, *fight a
  warship* in any meaningful sense.
