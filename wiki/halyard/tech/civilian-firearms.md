# Civilian firearms in 2348

Civilian firearms in 2348 are the technological
descendants of the early-twenty-first-century weapons
the campaign's players grew up familiar with. Two
centuries of refinement have produced more reliable,
more accurate, more energy-efficient weapons; the
basic mechanisms (chemical-propellant, projectile-
launching, recoil-operated or gas-operated, semi-
automatic or manual) are recognizably continuous with
2025 firearms.

The *substantial change* is regulatory. Civilian
firearms ownership in 2348 is uniformly more
restricted than in 2025; aboard spacecraft, the
restrictions are absolute. The political-cultural
posture toward civilian firearms varies by region
but the technical baseline is everywhere similar.

## Regulatory framework

### Earth

Most Earth jurisdictions allow civilian firearms
ownership under licensing regimes that vary in
strictness. The general pattern:

- **Long arms** (rifles, shotguns) are typically
  permitted with licensing
- **Handguns** are typically more restricted
- **Magazine capacity** is typically capped
- **Automatic fire** is typically prohibited for
  civilian ownership
- **Storage requirements** are extensive
- **Ammunition sales** are tracked

Specific jurisdictions:

- **Confederation (US-Canadian)** — relatively
  permissive by Earth standards; constitutional
  protections for ownership; substantial regional
  variation
- **European Union (residual)** — quite restrictive;
  most ownership requires demonstrated need
- **African Secretariat** — moderate; varies by
  national jurisdiction
- **Asian Secretariat** — variable; Japan and
  South Korea are restrictive, India and
  Indonesia are moderate, smaller states variable
- **Latin American states** — variable
- **Australasia** — restrictive; New Zealand
  particularly so

### Off-world

- **Luna** — restrictive; civilian ownership
  requires substantial justification; most ownership
  is hunting (yes, Luna has habitat-internal
  shooting clubs and limited surface hunting in
  controlled environments)
- **Mars** — moderate; reflects MCR republican
  traditions; Universal Military Training produces
  a substantial trained-population baseline
- **Belt** — variable; most stations have local
  rules; typical practice is non-restrictive in
  station habitat life but absolute prohibition on
  any vessel underway

### Aboard spacecraft

This matters for the campaign:

- **Civilian discharge of any firearm aboard a
  pressurized spacecraft** is essentially universally
  prohibited. The risks of pressure-loss are
  catastrophic; the legal liability is enormous;
  insurance underwriters require strict
  prohibition.
- **Carry of firearms aboard spacecraft** is
  typically restricted to authorized security
  personnel.
- **Storage of firearms aboard** is restricted to
  hardened arms lockers under specific authority
  (ship's master, security officer, or formally
  designated equivalent).

The *Halyard* has an arms locker. It is sealed by
default. The Security Officer (Halden Cross) has
keys; Captain Marsh has emergency override. The
arms locker contents include limited firearms (see
*Modified PDC Rounds* in House Rules) and other
specialty equipment.

## Weapon types

The categories of civilian firearms in 2348:

### Handguns

The standard civilian handgun is a refinement of the
twenty-first-century semi-automatic pistol:

- **Caliber range**: 9mm-equivalent through
  .45-caliber-equivalent (Earth standards) or
  the corresponding metric and specialty calibers
- **Action**: typically gas-operated semi-
  automatic; some recoil-operated; some specialty
  manual-action models
- **Capacity**: typically 12-17 rounds, with
  jurisdiction-specific limits
- **Sights**: smart-sight technology now standard
  on most production handguns (electronic
  reticles, range-finding, optionally ballistic
  computation)

Manufacturers: Several major Earth manufacturers
produce civilian handguns; substantial Mars and Belt
production exists; the market is competitive.

### Rifles

Civilian rifles range from hunting-grade (single-shot
or low-capacity bolt-action) through sport-shooting
(typically magazine-fed semi-automatic) to substantial
hunting-and-defense rifles:

- **Standard hunting rifles** — bolt-action or
  lever-action; calibers from .22-equivalent
  through large-game .375-equivalent
- **Sport rifles** — semi-automatic; typically
  intermediate calibers (.223, .308, similar)
- **Defense rifles** — typically civilian-legal
  semi-automatic versions of military designs;
  jurisdictionally controversial in many regions

### Shotguns

Civilian shotguns have changed least from 2025.
Pump-action and semi-automatic designs remain
standard. Specialty applications (less-lethal beanbag
rounds, breaching slugs) are widely available.

### Specialty civilian weapons

- **Hunting bows and crossbows** — surprisingly
  popular for civilian sport. Off-world hunting
  on Earth uses these substantially.
- **Compressed-gas pistols and rifles** — used
  for sport shooting in environments where
  chemical propellant is undesirable
- **Specialty hunting tools** — tranquilizer
  rifles for wildlife management, specialty
  shotguns for game, etc.

## Smart-sight technology

The major contemporary innovation: most civilian
firearms now incorporate *smart sights*:

- **Electronic reticles** — adjustable, low-light
  capable, automatic ballistic compensation
- **Range-finding** — laser or optical
- **Ballistic computation** — for medium-range
  shots
- **Target acquisition** — limited; assists with
  acquiring moving targets
- **Recording** — many smart sights record sight-
  picture; useful for hunting documentation, sport
  shooting, and (regulatorily) firearms-discharge
  accountability

Smart sights are *legal* in most jurisdictions; some
restrict specific features (target-acquisition is
regulated in some jurisdictions because of its
potential military applications).

## Aboard the *Halyard*

The *Halyard*'s arms locker contains:

- **2 modified PDC rifles** — the primary security
  weapons. Modified for shipboard use (frangible
  ammunition, limited overpenetration, precise
  recoil control).
- **3 sidearms** — issued for security personnel.
- **2 less-lethal launchers** — sonic and
  electroshock (covered in less-lethal entry).
- **Specialty equipment** — restraints,
  decontamination tools, related security gear.

Arms locker access: Cross has keys; Marsh has
emergency override; the Quartermaster has access for
maintenance. Discharge of any firearm aboard the
ship requires direct Captain authorization unless
the safety conditions explicitly require it.

## Notes for play

- **Civilian firearms are not the campaign's
  primary tool.** The Halyard expedition is not
  a combat operation; weapons exist for
  contingency rather than primary use.
- **Aboard-ship firearms discharge is a major
  event.** A PC who fires a weapon aboard the
  *Halyard* has produced a *significant
  operational and political situation*. This is
  not a casual action.
- **Most civilian PCs will not be carrying
  firearms.** Cross is the security officer; the
  Captain has access; other crew typically would
  not have weapons in normal circumstances.
- **The regulatory framework matters in
  backstory.** A PC with substantial firearms
  experience has likely been through formal
  training, military service, or a specific
  licensing pathway.
