# Hellas City

![Hellas Lake at summer, Capitol building visible on the western shore](https://halyard.atlas-sjsu.duckdns.org/art/mars-hellas-lake.png)

![Hellas City schematic — district layout around the lake](https://halyard.atlas-sjsu.duckdns.org/art/hellas-city-map.svg)

Hellas City is the capital of the Martian Congressional
Republic and, by most reasonable measures, the capital of
human Mars. The metropolitan area covers roughly 1,400
square kilometers along the western shore of the Hellas
Basin's permanent lake, holds 7.4 million residents, and
sits at -7,150 m below the Martian datum — the lowest, and
therefore atmospherically thickest, large urban site on
Mars.

## Site and atmosphere

The Hellas Basin is the largest impact crater on Mars (and
one of the largest in the solar system), 2,300 km in
diameter, with a floor 7 to 8 kilometers below the surrounding
plateau. The depth gives the basin a thicker atmospheric
layer than anywhere else on Mars; in 2348 the surface
pressure at Hellas Lake is 0.58 bar, equivalent to Earth at
about 4,400 m altitude.

This makes Hellas the most *liveable* large region on Mars.
A filter mask is rarely necessary at lake level in summer;
a respirator is not needed for any normal activity. Children
born in Hellas are noticeably less mask-trained than children
born in Tharsis or the Northern Lowlands.

The basin's permanent lake — formed by the slow
accumulation of water from the polar caps and the
atmospheric-engineering rainfall — is roughly 600 km across
at its widest and varies in depth from a few meters along
the northern shore to about 250 m at the basin's center. The
lake is brackish but supports a deliberately introduced
ecosystem of Earth-origin lake species (carp, perch, several
algal species, and an experimental population of the
Martian-native chemo-autotrophs that have begun to colonize
the lake-bottom sediment naturally).

The Hellas climate is the most Earth-like on Mars. Summer
highs reach +18°C; winter lows fall to -28°C. Snow falls in
the basin's northern reaches in winter; the southern shore
sees freezing rain. Storm patterns are recognizably Earth-like
with a long dust-storm season in late autumn that closes
outdoor operations for two to four weeks.

## Layout

Hellas City is laid out as a series of concentric crescents
along the lake's western shore, with the oldest district
(Founders' Crescent) closest to the lake and successive
generations of expansion arrayed inland.

### Founders' Crescent

The original 2074 settlement and the political center of the
Republic. Two kilometers wide along the lake; one kilometer
deep. Includes:

- **The Hellas Capitol** — the Republic's legislative
  building. A neoclassical structure built 2278–2284 in
  imported red sandstone. The Senate chamber and the
  Assembly chamber occupy the building's two wings; the
  central rotunda holds the Mars Atmospheric Authority's
  ceremonial offices. The building's south face has a
  three-story glass wall overlooking the lake.
- **The President's Residence** — a comparatively modest
  walled compound near the Capitol. The Republic's
  founders rejected a grand presidential residence on
  ideological grounds; the current building is a
  Georgian-style structure with a working farm attached
  (a deliberate symbolic gesture).
- **The Mars Naval Academy** — the prestige officer
  commissioning institution. 1,400 cadets in residence at
  any time. The Academy's quad faces the lake; cadets row
  on it in season.
- **The Founders' Memorial** — the cenotaph for the first
  generation of Mars colonists. Most public ceremonies
  begin or end here. The list of names runs 14,000 long.

### Mid-Crescent

The cultural and commercial heart. Built primarily 2090s
through 2160s. Includes:

- **The Hellas Symphony Hall** — built 2247, considered the
  finest performance space on Mars. The Mars Symphony's
  resident hall.
- **The University of Hellas** — Mars's oldest university,
  founded 2098. 64,000 students. The Faculty of
  Atmospheric Engineering is the most prestigious in human
  space; the Faculty of Republican Studies is the
  political-philosophy heartland of the Republic.
- **The Hellas Bourse** — Mars's primary commodity
  exchange. The standard reference price for Mars-origin
  manufactured goods.
- **The Capitol Market** — the largest of the city's open-air
  market districts. Famous for fish (lake-caught), produce
  (basin agriculture), and the imported delicacies that
  arrive by twice-daily Earth-Mars freighter.

### Outer Crescents

The residential and industrial districts, built primarily
2160s through present. Includes the major hospitals, the
heavy-industry zones (mostly to the north of the city),
and the dormitory neighborhoods that house most of the
working population.

### The Vapor District

A specialized eastern neighborhood near the lake's industrial
inlet, devoted to the atmospheric-engineering installations.
Not residential. The towers of the air-handling systems are
visible from anywhere in the city; some Hellasites navigate
by them.

## Politics

Hellas is the political center of the Republic. The Senate
sits here. The Assembly sits here. The Supreme Court sits
here. Most major federal agencies are headquartered here. The
city's political culture is therefore unusually dense — most
adults have personal connections to one or another part of
the federal apparatus.

The current Mayor is **Eduardo Mendiola**, a former MAA
deputy director. The City Council is split between two
parties — the **Republican Continuity** (centrist-conservative)
and the **Hellas Reform Caucus** (centrist-progressive) —
whose differences are mostly technocratic.

Hellas has a higher voter turnout than most Mars cities (84%
in the 2346 federal election; the Mars-wide average was 78%).

## Culture

The Hellas cultural style is distinctive even among Martians:

- **Formality.** Hellasites dress more formally than other
  Martians. Senate members wear morning coats during
  session; senior civil servants wear suits even on
  weekends. The lake-shore restaurants enforce dress codes
  that other Mars cities consider absurd.
- **Bibliophilia.** The University of Hellas's libraries
  hold the largest collection of pre-spaceflight texts on
  Mars. The lake-shore bookshops are a cultural
  institution. Most Hellasites read more than the Martian
  average; the Hellas Literary Review's annual best-of list
  is widely consulted.
- **The lake.** The lake structures civic life in ways
  Earthers find puzzling. Sailing, rowing, swimming
  (briefly, in summer), and lakeside walking are all
  major recreational activities. The lakeshore is publicly
  accessible by constitutional provision; private
  developments are not allowed within 200 m of the
  waterline.
- **The pre-secession nostalgia.** Hellas was the cultural
  center of Mars before secession; some of the older
  Hellas families still hold a quiet pre-Republican
  identity. They are not separatists. They are also not
  entirely on board with the Republic's nationalist style.

## Notable institutions

- **The Mars Atmospheric Authority headquarters**, in the
  Vapor District. The institutional center of terraforming.
- **The Hellas Naval Hospital**, the largest hospital on
  Mars. Operates the burn ward that handles all serious
  pressure-loss casualties from across the planet.
- **The Lake Authority**, the agency that manages the
  Hellas Lake's ecology, navigation, and water rights.
  Smaller than its name suggests but politically important.
- **The Republic's Library**, the central archive of the
  MCR. Operates a public reading room.
- **The Hellas Astronomical Observatory**, perched on
  Hellas Mons (the basin's eastern wall). Mars's principal
  ground-based optical observatory.

## Daily life

The average Hellas resident lives in a vertical district to
the inland of the lake (most residential construction is up,
in the Earth-arcology pattern, but lower — Hellas's tallest
residential towers are 600 m, half the height of Earth's
arcologies, because the smaller economy doesn't justify the
larger scale).

Transit is rail (the Hellas Metro, six lines, opened 2284
and continuously expanded), trolley (the lakeshore lines, a
deliberate cultural choice), and pedestrian. Private cars
are rare; cycling is widespread.

Food is regional Mars cuisine, which is its own thing:
heavily-vegetable, often-vegetarian (the early colonial diet
was vegetarian by economic necessity, and the cultural habit
stuck), with carp and lake fish as the major animal protein.
Imported delicacies — Earth wines, Belt-grown specialty
produce — are common in the higher tiers but not staple.

Religion: Hellas is the most religiously plural city on
Mars. The Hellas Cathedral (Catholic), the Hellas Mosque
(Sunni), the Hellas Lunar Temple (Buddhist), and the
Hellas Secular Humanist Hall are all within a 2 km radius
in Mid-Crescent.

## Hellas and the *Halyard*

Lt. Rae Kosugi's family lives in Hellas (her parents in the
Outer Crescent residential district; her sister at the
University). Kosugi grew up in Hellas, did her MCRN cadet
training at the Naval Academy, and considers Hellas her
permanent home.

If the campaign returns characters to Mars after Nithon —
which is one of the post-mission scenarios the Keeper may
develop — Hellas City is the most likely arrival point. The
*Halyard* could refit at Phobos, but a debriefing with MCRN
authorities or a personal visit by Kosugi to her family
would bring at least some of the crew to Hellas.

## Notes for play

- Hellas is sophisticated and a little snobbish. A Belter
  PC visiting Hellas should feel slightly out of place; an
  Earther PC will feel more at home but will find the
  Martian formality unfamiliar.
- The lake is a tonal asset. Scenes set on the lakeshore
  carry a different feeling than scenes in any other Mars
  setting — calmer, more contemplative, more Earth-like.
  Use it.
- The Naval Academy is a useful narrative anchor for any
  Mars-PC backstory and a useful setting for a flashback
  if the campaign needs one.
