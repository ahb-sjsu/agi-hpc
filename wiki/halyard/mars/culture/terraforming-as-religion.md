# Terraforming as religion

The Mars Atmospheric Authority's annual report opens, by
tradition, with a sentence drafted in 2284 and unchanged
since: *"The work of making a planet continues."* The
sentence is read aloud at every Mars elementary school's
opening assembly each year. It is on the back of the 50-
credit Republic note. It is on the lapel pin given to every
new MCRN ensign. It is also, in a way Earthers find
puzzling and Martians find self-evident, *the closest thing
the Republic has to a national religion.*

This entry tries to describe a phenomenon that does not
quite have an equivalent on Earth.

## What "religion" means here

Terraforming is not a religion in the sense of a metaphysical
commitment, an institutional church, or a ritual life. The
MCR is a constitutionally secular state with a religiously
plural population. About 18% of Martians are Catholic; about
14% are Sunni Muslim; about 11% are Buddhist (mostly Lunar
Temple lineage); about 8% are Hindu; about 6% are
Christian-Other; smaller fractions are other traditions; and
roughly 35% identify as secular or unaffiliated.

What terraforming-as-religion *is* is a **civic religion** in
the political-philosophical sense — a shared narrative of
collective purpose, a body of practices that reinforce that
narrative, and a moral economy organized around it. The
United States in the twentieth century had something
similar (the *American Project*); the early Soviet Union had
something similar (the *Construction of Socialism*); pre-
modern dynasties usually did (the *Mandate*). Mars has
*the Work*.

## The narrative

The narrative, as told to a Martian seven-year-old:

> Mars is a planet we are making. It was a dead world. Our
> ancestors began the work of making it alive. We are
> continuing the work. Our children will continue. Their
> children's children will continue. By the time it is
> finished, no one alive today will remember it was ever
> begun. That is the beauty of it.

The narrative as told in a Martian senate speech:

> The Republic is the political vehicle of a project larger
> than the Republic. Our institutions exist to serve the
> Work; the Work does not exist to serve our institutions.
> When the Work and the institutions conflict, the Work
> wins.

The narrative as told in a Tharsis bar at midnight:

> You think Earth understands this? They don't. They can't.
> They were *given* a world. We are *making* one.

The narrative is taught in school, performed in civic
ritual, threaded through the political discourse, and
internalized by most Martians as the structure of meaning
they live within. It does not exclude other religious or
philosophical commitments. Most Martians hold both — they
go to mass on Sunday, *and* they participate in the Work.

## The practices

A working Martian's life is shaped by the Work in concrete
ways:

### The atmospheric tithe

Every Martian wage-earner pays an *atmospheric tithe* — a
0.5% surcharge on income earmarked for the MAA's terraforming
budget. The tithe is voluntary in legal form (Martians can
opt out) but socially almost universal (about 96% of
eligible adults pay it).

The tithe is the most visible economic practice of the
civic religion. Opting out is not illegal but is a marker
that other Martians notice. The opt-out community is, in
most Martian cities, a recognized minority — usually older
Earth-born immigrants, libertarian intellectuals, or
adherents of one of the smaller separatist sects.

### The seasonal rituals

The MAA conducts public terraforming-related ceremonies
quarterly, tied to the Martian seasons:

- **Northern Spring** — the *Atmospheric Renewal*, a
  ceremony at the North Polar atmospheric station. Public.
  Televised. The Mars Symphony plays.
- **Northern Summer** — the *Forest Census*, when MAA
  ecologists tally the equatorial forest's growth. Public.
  Schools take field trips.
- **Northern Autumn** — the *Water Council*, when the
  Valles Marineris river management plan is updated.
  Civic, not ceremonial; widely reported.
- **Northern Winter** — the *Mars Day* (28 December
  Earth-equivalent), when a single moment of silence
  marks the founding of the Republic and the continuation
  of the Work.

These are not religious services. They are civic events.
They are also, observably, treated with the gravity that
religious services would be on Earth.

### The vocational identification

A Martian's introductory question to a stranger is *what is
your profession?* — and the question carries a weight Earth
does not give it. Profession is, for Martians, partly an
expression of one's role in the Work. An atmospheric
engineer is participating directly. A teacher is preparing
the next generation. A merchant is sustaining the economy
that funds the project. Even a poet is contributing — *the
Work needs witnesses.*

Earthers find this earnestness slightly oppressive. Most
Martians do not.

### The civic liturgy

The Martian primary-school day begins, in most provinces,
with a short reading from the MAA's annual report. Secondary
schools have a weekly *terraforming history* class. University
graduation includes a recitation of *the founding sentences*.
The MCRN's basic training has a unit specifically on the
relation of military service to the Work.

The liturgy is mostly secular in form. Religious Martians
sometimes layer their own traditions onto it; secular
Martians take it on its own terms.

## The political consequences

The civic religion has political consequences that shape
modern Mars:

### Long-horizon governance

Martian political debates are framed in centuries, not
electoral cycles. A four-year-elected Senator is expected to
think on a hundred-year scale. Tax policy is debated in
terms of long-term terraforming feasibility. Environmental
disputes are settled by reference to the Work's needs.
Education policy is shaped by the requirement to produce the
specialists the Work will need in the next generation.

This is a strength when the issue is genuinely long-term. It
is a weakness when an immediate crisis demands attention and
the political system is unable to focus.

### The weight given to the MAA

The Mars Atmospheric Authority is the institutional
embodiment of the civic religion. Its budget is the largest
single line item in the federal budget. Its director is, by
custom, an apolitical career civil servant. Its decisions are
treated with deference that other agencies do not receive.

The MAA's political power has, on occasion, been a problem.
The 2326 *MAA Spending Crisis* — when the Authority's
director refused to comply with congressional budget cuts on
the grounds that the cuts would damage long-term terraforming
goals — produced a constitutional standoff that ended only
when the President personally intervened. The Authority lost
the budget fight but retained its moral authority.

### The skepticism toward Earth

Earth, as a finished world that did not need to be made, is
the opposite of Mars. Earthers, in the Martian view, do not
understand what it is to participate in a project. The
post-Compact Earther melancholy is, for Martians, evidence
of what happens to a society without the Work. Many Martians
regard Earth with a particular kind of pity that Earth finds
condescending.

This is, of course, partly cope. Mars also has its
melancholies; the Work is a way of structuring against them
rather than a guarantee against them. But the Mars-Earth
asymmetry on this point is real and visible to anyone who
spends time in both cultures.

### The dignity given to manual work

Mars's working class — the engineers, the technicians, the
maintenance workers, the farmers — has a cultural prestige
that Earth's working class lost in the late twenty-first
century. A Tharsis pipefitter is not, in Martian terms, a
person without higher prospects; she is a person whose
prospects are exactly what they should be. The Work needs
pipefitters.

This is, on Earth, sometimes dismissed as Martian propaganda.
On Mars it is a fact of life. A Tharsis pipefitter looks an
Earther doctorate-holder in the eye without difficulty.

## What it does not provide

The civic religion of terraforming is good at giving Martians
purpose. It is not good at giving them *meaning*. Terraforming
does not answer the question *what should I do with my one
life*; it answers *what should our society do with the next
ten generations*. The two are related but not identical.

Religious Martians (the 65% who hold a personal religious
commitment in addition to the civic one) report this gap and
fill it with their tradition. Secular Martians fill it with
the standard menu of contemporary humanism — relationships,
art, achievement, the cultivation of inner life.

The Earther melancholy does not afflict Mars not because
terraforming solves the problem but because terraforming
*frames* the problem differently — as a question about the
specific shape of an obviously meaningful life rather than as
a question about whether life can have meaning at all.

## The terraforming and the *Halyard* contract

This is the harder question. A Martian crew member on the
*Halyard* contract — Kosugi, Korvaly, Vorstov in their
different ways — is in some sense leaving the Work to do
something else. The Work will be there when they get back;
they will not have moved it forward; they will have spent
two years of their participation on a project that has
nothing to do with Mars.

The justification, for each of them, will be different and
each of them will have it ready. *Earning experience the
Work will eventually need.* *Investigating something that
might affect Mars's strategic situation.* *The money funds
my children's atmospheric education.* *Curiosity is also a
form of participation.* All true, all partial.

Most Martian deep-range contractors come back with a quiet
dissonance — they have done good work, they have been
well-paid, and they have not Worked. The dissonance does not
usually break them, but it changes them.

## Notes for play

- A Martian PC's relationship to the civic religion is
  worth establishing in backstory. Devout? Skeptical?
  Reformist? Quietly opted out? All are playable; each
  shapes how the character handles the *Halyard* contract.
- Don't make every Martian into a Work-evangelist. Most
  Martians take the civic religion as oxygen — present,
  vital, unremarked.
- The Earther melancholy, encountered on the *Halyard*,
  will look strange to a Martian crewmate. They have not
  seen it from the inside. The Earther crewmates will read
  Martian seriousness as a failure of irony.
- When the campaign reaches the cosmic-horror beats, the
  Martian framework — *we are participating in something
  larger than ourselves; the project takes generations; the
  meaning is in the work* — will turn out to be unexpectedly
  useful. Or unexpectedly broken. Keeper's call.
