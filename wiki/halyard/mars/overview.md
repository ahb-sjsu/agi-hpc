# Mars in 2348 — overview

Mars in 2348 is two hundred years into terraforming and
twelve thousand kilometers ahead of where Earth thinks it
should be. The atmosphere is breathable in the lowlands with
a filter mask. The Hellas basin has open water in summer.
There are forests at the equator that nobody planted, where
the introduced rhizobacteria and the slow oxygenation have
allowed slow seedings of cold-adapted Earth flora to take.
Mars is the planet that decided it was a planet and has spent
two centuries proving the point.

## Population

Mars has 980 million people, distributed across roughly thirty
major cities and a long tail of smaller settlements. The
demographic distribution:

- **Hellas Basin** — about 280 million people. The political,
  industrial, and cultural center of Mars. Hellas City (the
  capital), Lyot, Crocco.
- **Tharsis Plateau** — about 220 million. Olympus, Pavonis,
  Ascraeus, Arsia. The original colonial heartland;
  industrially dominant; the MCRN's principal naval bases.
- **Valles Marineris** — about 180 million. Coprates, Melas,
  Candor, Eos. The trade and transit corridor that runs
  east-to-west across most of the equator. Boulevard cities,
  riverbarge culture (the Valles has *rivers* now).
- **Northern Lowlands** — about 160 million. Acidalia,
  Utopia, Arcadia. The new frontier; rapidly growing; mostly
  agriculturalist.
- **Southern Highlands** — about 100 million. The conservative
  cultural region; oldest settlements (Hellas-east); the
  martial tradition.
- **The Polar Caps** — about 40 million. Industrial and
  scientific. The Mars Exploration Reserve runs out of South
  Polar; the major water reserves and atmospheric monitoring
  stations are at North Polar.

Mars is younger than Earth demographically. The median age is
38 (vs. Earth's 46). The childhood population is larger; the
elderly population smaller. Mars is, in this sense, the
youngest of the human polities — if you don't count the Belt,
which is younger still.

## Atmosphere and climate

The atmospheric pressure at the Martian datum is 0.41 bar (as
of 2347), up from the 0.006 bar of the pre-terraforming
baseline. The atmosphere is 81% nitrogen, 16% oxygen, 1.5%
argon, with the balance trace gases. The composition is
deliberate; Martian atmospheric engineering favors a higher
nitrogen fraction than Earth's because of the lower-pressure
environment (the partial pressure of oxygen at Mars datum is
roughly equivalent to Earth at 4,000 m altitude).

Surface temperatures range from -55°C (winter, polar regions)
to +18°C (summer, Hellas basin lowlands). The seasons are
twice as long as Earth's; a Martian year is 687 Earth-days.

A *filter mask* is the standard Martian outdoor wear. Most
adults carry one folded into a pocket; most children below
ten wear them habitually outdoors. The masks filter dust and
boost oxygen concentration; they are unnecessary for survival
in lowland summer but advisable. Highland and winter outdoor
travel still requires more substantial respiratory equipment.

The native Martian dust storms still occur. They are no longer
planet-spanning (the higher atmospheric mass damps them) but
a regional storm can shut down outdoor operations for two or
three weeks. Martians have an entire vocabulary of storm
behaviors that Earthers find baffling.

## Government

Mars is the **Martian Congressional Republic**, a federal
democracy of 24 constituent provinces. The structure mirrors
American federalism in an obvious and intentional way — the
2272 founding generation modeled the constitution on U.S. and
Indian precedents — but the political culture has diverged
substantially.

Key institutions:

- **The Congress** — bicameral. The Senate has four members
  per province; the Assembly is apportioned by population.
  Both chambers serve four-year terms; one-third of the
  Senate rotates every two years.
- **The Presidency** — directly elected, six-year term, single
  term limit. The current President (2344–2350) is **Elena
  Voss-Tanaka**, a former MCRN admiral and the third
  ex-military President of the post-secession era.
- **The Supreme Court** — 11 justices, lifetime appointments,
  selected by the President with Senate confirmation. The
  Court has historically been the most independent of the
  three branches.
- **The Civil Service** — non-partisan, professional. Roughly
  340,000 employees, organized into 24 ministries.
- **The MCRN** — the navy. Effectively the fourth branch of
  government, with a constitutional autonomy that Earth's
  UNN does not have.

The political culture differs from Earth in three ways:

1. **Higher participation.** Voter turnout in MCR elections
   averages 78%, vs. 41% in UN federal elections. Martians
   take politics seriously.
2. **More martial.** The MCRN's prestige in the political
   class is high. Three of the last six Presidents are
   ex-MCRN. The military-civilian distinction is more porous
   than on Earth.
3. **More terraform-oriented.** The Mars project is a
   collective enterprise that organizes most large political
   debates. Tax policy is debated as terraforming policy.
   Education is debated as terraforming policy. Even foreign
   policy is debated as terraforming policy. The unspoken
   premise is that Mars exists to become a habitable planet,
   and everything else is in service of that goal.

## Economy

Mars's economy is roughly $14 trillion UN-equivalent, with
the following structure (rough):

- **Atmospheric engineering and terraforming** — 18% of GDP.
  The largest single sector. State, private, and joint
  enterprise.
- **Manufacturing (heavy)** — 14%. The Tharsis industrial
  complex, the orbital industries.
- **Agriculture** — 11%. The Northern Lowlands are now
  net-food-exporters.
- **Mining and resource extraction** — 9%. Phobos, Deimos,
  the Northern Polar reserves.
- **Defense** — 8%. The MCRN consumes roughly 6% of GDP
  directly and another 2% indirectly through procurement.
- **Services** — 28%. Education, healthcare, culture, tourism,
  finance.
- **Trade** — 12%. Mostly with the Belt, secondarily with
  Earth.

Mars is rich. The per-capita GDP is roughly 78% of Earth's,
but the cost of living is lower; most Martians live more
comfortably in absolute terms than most Earthers, despite the
lower headline figure.

There is no equivalent of the General Provision on Mars. The
MCR has a comprehensive welfare state — universal healthcare,
universal education, a minimum income for the elderly and
disabled — but the working-age population is expected to work
for income. The Martian objection to the Provision is
ideological: a republic of citizens should have something to
contribute. Earth's Provision is regarded, by most Martians,
as the symptom of a civilizational decline.

## Culture

The signature elements:

- **The terraforming sensibility.** Most Martians feel that
  they participate in a project larger than themselves. This
  is not metaphor; the public-school curriculum integrates
  terraforming history and policy from primary school onward.
- **The republican civic culture.** Mars retains a strong
  civic-republican tradition that Earth has largely lost.
  Town meetings, citizen assemblies, jury service, military
  reserve service — all have higher participation rates than
  on Earth.
- **The martial tradition.** Mars's defense posture is
  serious. Universal military training (8 weeks at age 18)
  exists; reserve service is common; the MCRN is a
  career-defining institution for many who never make it a
  career.
- **The frontier sensibility.** Mars still feels like a
  frontier in a way that Earth has not for centuries. The
  Northern Lowlands have a recognizable pioneer culture; the
  South Polar science stations have a heroic-explorer culture;
  the Tharsis industrial cities have a builder-engineer
  culture.
- **The Earth-suspicion.** Most Martians regard Earth with
  the wary fondness of a former colony for its former
  metropole. Earthers are tolerated, not entirely trusted,
  occasionally welcomed, regularly mocked.
- **The Belter solidarity.** Mars's relationship with the
  Belt is complicated. There is a strong strain of pro-Belt
  sentiment in Martian culture (we are both off-world; we
  both have been cheated by Earth). There is a counter-strain
  of Mars-superior thinking (we are a republic; the Belt is a
  patchwork). Both strains coexist.

## Mars and the *Halyard*

The *Halyard* is not a Martian ship. It is Belt-flagged. But
two of the *Halyard*'s standing crew are Martian:

- **Lt. Rae Kosugi**, lander pilot, MCRN reservist. Mars-born,
  Mars-educated. Returns to Mars between contracts; her
  family is in Hellas City.
- **Maren Korvaly**, second engineer. Mars-born, Earth-educated,
  Belt-employed. The complicated path; not unusual.

Two more *Halyard* crew have substantive Mars connections:

- **Ark Vorstov**, first officer. Born on Mars to a Russian
  émigré family; UNN officer (rare for a Martian-born); now
  contracts widely.
- **Captain Marsh**, who served two MCRN-UNN joint exercises
  and has personal relationships with several MCRN officers
  she may need to call on.

## Notes for play

- A Mars-born PC is more politically engaged, more physically
  active, and more skeptical of Earth than an Earth-born PC
  by default. These are stereotypes; play against them when
  the character demands it.
- The "second reason" for taking the *Halyard* contract is
  different for a Mars-born PC. Mars does not produce the
  Earther melancholy. Mars-born contractors are usually
  there for *the work itself*, the *career*, or *the
  curiosity*.
- Don't make Martian PCs into MCRN propaganda. The political
  culture is real but most Martians are not partisans.
