# Religion on Earth, 2348

The mid-twenty-first century was widely (and wrongly) predicted
to be the end of religion as a serious force in human culture.
The post-Compact era has put that prediction to bed. Religion
in 2348 is neither dominant nor disappearing. It is one of the
quieter components of what Earthers do with their lives, and
it has — surprising the secular intellectuals of two centuries
ago — slowly grown rather than shrunk in the century since the
Late Crisis ended.

This entry is the broad summary; particular traditions get
their own files (`earth/culture/religion-<tradition>.md`).

## The pattern

Earth in 2348 is religiously plural in a way that 2025 would
recognize and appreciate. The largest traditions, in order:

- **Christianity** — about 28% of UN citizens identify as
  Christian, down from a 2025 peak but stabilizing. Heaviest
  in the Americas and Africa. Catholic majority, with
  significant Orthodox, Anglican, and post-evangelical
  Protestant communities.
- **Islam** — about 24% of UN citizens. Heaviest in North
  Africa, the Middle East, South and Southeast Asia. The
  Sunni-Shi'a-Ibadi distinctions remain meaningful at the
  community level but have receded as objects of formal
  political conflict.
- **Hinduism and South Asian religions** — about 15% of UN
  citizens. Heaviest in South Asia and the South Asian
  diaspora. The Hindu reform movements of the late twenty-
  first century have produced a tradition that is, by 2348,
  more philosophically articulate and less ritually obsessive
  than its pre-modern ancestor.
- **Buddhism** — about 9% of UN citizens, with regional
  concentrations in East and Southeast Asia. The Lunar Temple
  tradition (a synthesis of Theravada, Zen, and lunar-station
  monasticism) has been the major innovation of the past
  century and is the fastest-growing Buddhist branch.
- **Indigenous and traditional religions** — about 5% of UN
  citizens. The Ifá Revival, Vodou, the various First Nations
  traditions in the Americas, the Aboriginal and Maori
  traditions in Oceania. Often practiced in hybrid with one
  of the larger traditions.
- **Secular humanism / secular Buddhism / philosophical
  fellowships** — about 11% of UN citizens identify as
  *spiritually engaged but not religious*. The Secular
  Humanist Fellowships, founded 2278, are the largest formal
  organization in this category.
- **Unaffiliated** — about 8% of UN citizens, sometimes called
  the *Quiet Citizens*. Neither religious nor explicitly
  secular; just not engaged with the question.

These numbers add to a hundred. The questions that prompted
them are leading; the realities are messier; many Earthers
participate in more than one tradition or rotate through
several over a lifetime.

## What changed

The post-Compact growth of religion is interesting and worth
naming directly. It is not a return to nineteenth-century
piety. It is something else.

Three forces:

**The Earther melancholy.** Religion is one of the major
cultural responses to the post-Compact existential question.
The Provision settles material need; it does not settle
purpose. Religious traditions, by offering a thick account of
purpose that the secular institutions cannot provide, fill a
real gap. This is not naive — most contemporary Earthers who
go to church or temple know what they are doing and why.

**The contemplative turn.** The Lunar Temple tradition,
specifically, has had an outsized cultural effect on Earth.
The model — a structured contemplative practice without a
strong metaphysical commitment, oriented toward inner
stability rather than salvation — fits the cultural moment.
Many *non-Buddhist* Earthers now do twenty minutes of seated
meditation a day.

**The off-world frame.** Two centuries of expanding human
presence in space have, paradoxically, made religion more
plausible rather than less. The universe revealed by deep
astronomy is large, strange, and indifferent in ways that
make religious accounts of human meaning *more* compelling to
some Earthers, not less. The classic argument from atheists —
"the universe is too vast to be a stage for divine
attention" — turned out, in the long run, to cut both ways.

## Christianity in 2348

The Catholic Church is the largest single religious institution
on Earth. Headquartered in the rebuilt Vatican (Rome was
extensively storm-damaged in the 2197 floods and rebuilt under
the 2204–2231 Renaissance Restoration program), the Church has
about a billion adherents, a smaller off-world presence (the
Lunar diocese, the Martian diocese, several Belt missions),
and a long-running internal tension between its older European
and Latin American constituencies and its newer African and
Asian ones.

The Catholic Church has been theologically innovative on the
question of *non-human persons*. The 2298 encyclical
*Humani Dignitas Stellaris* established that, should
intelligent non-human life be discovered, the Church's
position would be that such beings are creatures of God and
"objects of God's regard, in a manner the Church does not
presume to specify in advance." The encyclical has been the
subject of constant theological commentary; most contemporary
Catholic philosophers consider it a placeholder rather than
a settled doctrine.

The Protestant denominations are smaller and more
fragmented. The *post-evangelical* movement — the surviving
remnant of twenty-first-century American evangelicalism, much
chastened — is concentrated in the Americas and parts of
Africa. The Anglican Communion remains the establishment
church of the European cultural museum.

Orthodox Christianity has had a quiet renaissance in the
former Russian sphere and parts of the Balkans, with a
flourishing monastic tradition that draws spiritual tourists
from across Earth.

## Islam in 2348

Islam in the post-Compact era has consolidated around several
intellectual centers — Cairo, Tehran, Istanbul, Jakarta, Lagos —
and has been theologically active on questions of governance,
science, and bioethics.

The most significant institutional development of the past
century is the **Council of the Confluences**, an informal
network of senior Sunni and Shi'a scholars that meets
quarterly to coordinate responses to questions affecting all
Muslims. The Council has no formal authority but its
consensus statements are widely respected.

Like the Catholic Church, the Islamic scholarly tradition has
considered the question of non-human persons. The 2304
*Statement of the Confluences* held that "the Creator's
attention is not exhausted by what we have found, and what is
found should be regarded with the courtesy proper to a
creature of God." Both the Catholic and Islamic positions
echo each other. Neither tradition has had to apply its
theology to a specific case.

## Buddhism and the Lunar Temple

Buddhism's growth has been concentrated in the Lunar Temple
tradition, which originated as a station-monastic adaptation
of Theravada and Zen practice in the early Lunar
industrialization period.

The Lunar Temple is now a recognized lineage with about 400
million adherents Earth-wide and a substantial Lunar and
Belt presence. Its monastic practice retains the strict
discipline of older Buddhisms. Its lay practice is unusual:
short, frequent, integrated with industrial work, and
markedly less metaphysically committed than the source
traditions.

The Lunar Temple's defining contribution to Earth culture
has been the *seated minute* — a one-minute meditation
intended to be done at intervals throughout the day, often
at chimes integrated into station and city public-address
systems. Many Earthers who would not call themselves Buddhist
do the seated minute when prompted.

## The Ifá Revival

The Ifá Revival is a deliberate reconstruction and
modernization of West African Ifá tradition, centered in
Lagos and the Yoruba diaspora. The Revival began in the
2310s as a cultural reclamation movement; by 2348 it has
matured into a recognized contemporary tradition with
several million practitioners and a growing presence in the
African Secretariat's cultural establishment.

The Revival's philosophical thrust is *destiny under
agency* — a doctrine that human beings are born with a
trajectory but retain meaningful choice over how it is
lived. The Revival's ritual life is rich; the divinatory
practice (the casting of palm nuts, the consultation of the
Ifá corpus) is taken seriously by adherents and treated with
respectful curiosity by outsiders.

## Secular fellowships

The Secular Humanist Fellowships are the closest thing to an
organized atheist tradition on Earth. Their meetings combine
contemplative practice with discussion of philosophy, science,
and ethics. About 8 million people attend Fellowship meetings
regularly worldwide.

Most Quiet Citizens — the unaffiliated 8% — are not
philosophically committed atheists. They are people who
simply do not engage with the religious question.

## What religion does not do, in 2348

Religion in 2348 is not, on Earth, a major political force.
The political-religious movements of the late twenty-first
century mostly burned out by 2230. The major religious
institutions are politically engaged but neither dominant nor
militant. There is no twenty-first-century-style religious
nationalism in any major UN polity, and the few attempts to
revive it have been politically marginal.

This is not because religion is weaker. It is because the
political settlement of the post-Compact era — the Provision,
the federal-tax authority, the multilingual cultural
policy — successfully *de-fused* religion from politics. The
churches and temples and mosques are full. The political
parties don't fight about them.

## Religion and the *Halyard* contract

A character's religious life is part of their backstory and,
if it is meaningful, part of their texture. The Catholic
chaplaincy on Ceres will visit the *Halyard* before
departure if a Catholic crew member requests it; the
equivalent service exists for Sunni and Shi'a Muslim crew
through the Mao-Kwikowski cultural office; Buddhist crew can
arrange a Lunar Temple farewell on the inbound leg through
Luna.

The major religious traditions all hold positions on the
discovery of non-human life that, in principle, would
accommodate it. Whether those positions hold up under contact
is another question.

The Keeper's note: do not use a character's religion as a
*weapon* against them in horror. Use it as a resource. A
character with serious religious commitment has a frame for
processing the impossible that the secular characters do
not. This will, eventually, matter.

## Notes for play

- A character with strong religious commitment is unusual but
  not strange in 2348. About 35% of UN citizens describe
  themselves as religiously committed in some serious way.
- Religious practice is mostly private and quiet. Public
  religious display is rare, slightly gauche in most settings,
  and unremarkable in others (Lagos, parts of South Asia).
- Most religious traditions have made some institutional
  preparation for the discovery of non-human persons. The
  preparation is theological, not pastoral. The pastoral
  preparation will have to be invented in real time.
