# The General Provision

The General Provision is the basic-income compact that has been
the load-bearing political settlement of Earth's twenty-third
century. Every UN citizen — by virtue of being a citizen, with
no work test and no means test — is entitled to:

- A monthly cash payment of **1,400 UN credits**, indexed to
  regional cost-of-living
- A **housing voucher** sufficient to rent an unfurnished single
  unit in a Tier-3 vertical district (or a partial subsidy
  toward a higher tier)
- **Universal health coverage** under the Convergence standard
- **Educational entitlement** through the equivalent of a
  bachelor's degree, taken at any age, in any field

The Provision is not a poverty line; it is the floor of
participation. It is enough to live. It is not enough to live
*well* in a top-tier megacity. Most Earthers receive Provision
plus contract work; a minority (the Strata, top professionals,
some long-term contract holders) opt out and pay an extra-tier
tax instead.

## Why it exists

The Provision originated as a pragmatic settlement, not an
ideological one. By the 2280s, three converging realities had
made some form of universal guarantee politically inevitable:

1. **The labor displacement curve.** A century of automation,
   demographic decline, and reorganization had hollowed out the
   middle of most occupational hierarchies. Ten percent of the
   workforce produced eighty percent of the value-add; the rest
   of the population needed a way to be in the economy without
   being economically necessary.
2. **The political price of consolidation.** The UN's federal
   ambitions required mass legitimacy. The Provision was the
   politically saleable face of consolidation — the argument
   that "you give up some sovereignty to the UN, and in
   exchange the UN guarantees the floor."
3. **The administrative simplicity of universality.** Means-
   tested welfare had become an absurdity by the 2280s — the
   administrative cost of policing eligibility exceeded the
   cost of paying everyone. Universality cut through the
   tangle.

The Provision Compact (Geneva, 2291) gave the UN direct fiscal
authority for the program, financed by:

- A federal income tax on incomes above the regional median
  (progressive, capped at 38% on the highest bracket)
- A wealth tax on net assets above 12 million UN credits
  (1.5% annual, with allowances for primary residence and
  productive enterprise)
- A consumption tax on luxury goods (variable rate, set
  regionally; the Lunar Resort tax is famous for being 60%)
- A federal share of corporate profit tax (8% baseline,
  surcharges for resource-extraction industries)

The Strata pay most of the wealth tax. The Comfortable pay most
of the income tax. The Working Provisioned and Off-Roll pay the
consumption tax (which is why luxury goods are gauche on
working-class Earth — even small purchases trigger registration
visible in your fiscal record).

## How it works in daily life

A Working Provisioned Earther's monthly cycle looks roughly
like this:

- **1st**: Provision deposit clears. Standing housing voucher
  applies to rent. Health coverage is automatic.
- **3rd–7th**: Bills, savings, family transfers.
- **8th–28th**: Contract income (if any). Most contracts are
  weekly or per-project. The work-lottery system handles
  bidding for better-paid contracts.
- **29th–31st**: Tax adjustments and transfer reconciliation.

For a contractor — most outer-system specialists, including
most members of the *Halyard*'s crew — the Provision is the
floor. You take Provision. You bid for contracts. You compete
for outer-system postings, which pay extravagantly. You return
to Earth (if you do) richer than you would otherwise have been.

Most Earthers spend between three and twelve months out of any
given year on contract work, depending on skill, demand, and
preference. The minority who never contract are mostly the very
old, the disabled, or the genuinely uninterested in income above
floor.

## The work-lottery

Officially named the **Federal Contract Allocation System**, the
work-lottery is the queue mechanism by which most contract
opportunities are allocated. The system is, on paper, an open
bidding platform: contractors set availability and skill
profiles, employers set requirements, the system matches and
runs an auction. In practice, contractors with strong skill
profiles are matched first, and the auction is a formality.

The lottery handles roughly 70% of all contract work on Earth.
The remaining 30% is allocated outside the system — direct hire,
referral, professional networks. The off-system route is what
the Comfortable use; the lottery is what most Working
Provisioned interact with.

The outer-system tier of the lottery is the top of the queue.
Contracts there pay 4–10x baseline rates because nobody wants
them. Most members of the *Halyard*'s crew are in this tier.

## Politics

The Provision is the most politically stable settlement on
Earth. Even the parties that hate it — the Sovereigntist
Federation on the right, the Provision-Plus Coalition on the
left — agree it cannot be dismantled. The fights are around the
margins: the indexing formula, the wealth-tax threshold, the
extra-tier opt-out, the cap on regional variance.

There is one specific political fight worth flagging because
it touches the campaign:

- **The Outer-System Surcharge debate.** The UN Outer Authority
  has proposed (in 2342, 2344, and 2347) extending Provision
  coverage to long-term outer-system residents. The proposal
  loses each time, narrowly. The argument against: Provision
  was a settlement among Earthers; outer-system residents are
  participating in a different economy. The argument for:
  Earth still claims jurisdiction, and a Provision floor would
  reduce Belter and Lunar emigration. The fight will eventually
  be won by the second argument. It has not been yet.

For your characters: if they're Earth nationals, they receive
Provision. While on the *Halyard* contract, the Provision
deposits keep arriving (it's automatic, no work test). When
they return — *if* they return — that floor is still there.
This is the single most important fact about Earther
psychology in 2348: there is a floor. You can take a contract
to the edge of the heliopause and not be afraid of starving on
the way home.

## Critiques

Three substantial critiques of the Provision exist, none
politically dominant:

- **The Sovereigntist critique** — the Provision is a federal
  intrusion on national sovereignty; the federal tax authority
  was the camel's nose under the tent. Vocal among older
  Americans, some Russian regions.
- **The Provision-Plus critique** — the floor is too low. A
  Provision should be enough to live well, not enough to live.
  Vocal among urban Africans, parts of South America, the
  European left.
- **The Off-Worlder critique** — the Provision is a luxury that
  Earth can afford because of the labor-extraction relationship
  with Mars and the Belt. Earth maintains its standard of living
  partly by not paying full price for off-world labor. This
  view is widespread off Earth; almost unheard-of on Earth.

The third critique is, in the Keeper's view, the most accurate.
Earth in 2348 lives on its grandparents' inheritance and the
labor of distant cousins. Most Earthers do not think about this.
Off-worlders do.

## Notes for play

- An Earther character takes the Provision as oxygen — they're
  not grateful for it, they expect it. Belters and Martians
  find this attitude irritating.
- The Provision is the answer to "why didn't your character do
  something safer?" It's not destitution that pushes Earthers
  to the outer system. It's *boredom* and the lottery prize.
- A character whose Provision was suspended (rare; usually only
  for federal felony convictions or for fraud) would be a
  particular kind of person. Not impossible. Not the default.
