# The United Nations Navy — overview

The United Nations Navy is the largest military force in human
history by tonnage and the second-largest by personnel (the
MCRN, smaller in tonnage, has a slightly larger officer corps
per ton of warship). It is also, by inclination and doctrine,
primarily a coercive-diplomacy force — a navy built for
*presence* rather than for war-fighting. UNN capital ships are
overbuilt, under-maintained, and numerous. Its crews are
well-paid and professionally competent, and the MCRN has
beaten the UNN in every serious war-game since 2331.

## Origin

The UNN coalesced into something like its modern form by 2289.
Earlier UN naval forces were patchwork — multinational
contributions, leased hulls, port-of-convenience flagging. The
2284–2289 reforms (the Geneva Naval Conventions) gave the UN
direct command, federal procurement, and a unified officer
corps. The first all-UNN-built ship, the **UNS Vasa Mendoza**,
launched in 2291.

The UNN inherited a doctrine of *forward presence* from the
US-led twentieth-century traditions, modified for the realities
of three-dimensional space and Epstein-drive logistics. Where a
twenty-first-century carrier strike group could project power
into a region for a few months at a time, a UNN deep-range task
group is built around a flagship that can sustain a presence at
the orbits of Jupiter or Saturn for *years*. The trade-off:
flexibility for endurance.

## Structure

The UNN's command structure has three operational tiers:

### Inner System Command

Earth, Luna, and the inner Lagrange points. The bulk of UNN
tonnage. Largely a deterrent and ceremonial force; the heavy
work is the political signaling between Earth and Mars. Inner
System Command operates two carrier strike groups (centered on
the **Tsavo** and **Tassili** classes), plus the standing Lunar
patrol.

### Outer System Auxiliary

The OSA is the UNN component that any deep-range crew is
likely to encounter. It maintains:

- **Sensor beacons** along the major ecliptic transits. The
  beacons are passive listeners; they relay anomalies to OSA
  command in real-time minus light-speed delay. There are
  roughly 6,400 beacons in active service.
- **Rescue capability.** Three OSA cruiser-class hulls are
  positioned at Jupiter, Saturn, and the Trans-Neptunian
  trailing point. Their primary job is to respond to distress
  calls. They can be on-station at most outer-system
  destinations within 30–45 days under crash-burn.
- **Interdiction Squadrons.** Corvettes and frigates that
  patrol regulated transits. Their mandate is enforcement of
  UN §44.7 rules on hazardous contracts (cargo manifests,
  crew certification, environmental disclosures). The
  squadrons are small but active; a deep-range vessel will
  typically be hailed by an OSA interdiction unit at least
  once per outbound leg.
- **The Signal Corps.** OSA's most numerous and most boring
  branch. Uniformed, present at every major port and station,
  responsible for the day-to-day administrative interface
  between the UN and the outer-system economy.

### Special Operations Wing

The UNN's classified arm. Doctrine and order of battle are not
public. The wing is rare in the field, opaque in operations,
and bad news if you are its business. A deep-range crew might
encounter Special Ops in the form of an apparently-civilian
courier who happens to have very specific questions.

## Tonnage and order of battle (public figures)

Per the 2347 UN Defense Disclosure:

- **Carriers / Capital ships**: 28 (mixed *Tsavo*, *Tassili*,
  *Habsburg* classes)
- **Cruisers**: 113
- **Frigates and corvettes**: 412
- **Destroyers**: 196
- **Patrol craft and auxiliaries**: 2,140+
- **Reserve fleet (mothballed)**: roughly 800 hulls of various
  types, mostly at the L4 reserve depot

The disclosed tonnage is 14.2 million tons. The MCRN, by
comparison, discloses 9.8 million tons but has a markedly
better tonnage-to-combat-effectiveness ratio.

## Officer culture

The UNN's officer corps draws from across UN-Earth, with three
distinguishable cultural sub-streams:

- **The Old Service.** Officers from former-superpower
  successor states (US, China, India, Russia) whose families
  have been military for generations. Conservative,
  traditionalist, present in the senior ranks.
- **The Compact Generation.** Officers commissioned after the
  Geneva Conventions, whose loyalty is to the UNN rather than
  any inherited national tradition. The dominant culture in
  middle ranks.
- **The Outer Authority Track.** A specialized career path
  centered on outer-system service. Less glamorous,
  professionally demanding, the route by which someone like
  the *Halyard*'s captain (Iona Marsh, ex-UNN Outer Auxiliary)
  builds a reputation.

UNN officers are distinguished from MCRN officers, in the
mutual stereotype, by being slightly older, slightly less fit,
and substantially better-read.

## Doctrine

UNN doctrine is summarized internally as **the Three
Presences**:

1. **Diplomatic presence** — the UNN as the visible projection
   of UN authority into contested space. Ships are scheduled
   for port visits, ceremonial appearances, joint exercises
   with Lunar and Belt forces. The flag matters.
2. **Deterrent presence** — the UNN's carrier groups in the
   inner system as the ultima ratio of UN-Mars relations. The
   doctrine assumes nuclear deterrence is reliable; the UNN's
   conventional capability is to absorb a first strike and
   respond with controlled escalation.
3. **Constabulary presence** — the OSA's enforcement of UN
   trade and environmental rules in the outer system. Less
   politically visible but operationally most important.

The doctrine deprioritizes *force-on-force naval combat*. The
UNN has not fought a fleet engagement since the brief
Mars-Earth War of 2279. War-games against the MCRN have, since
2331, tended to result in MCRN victories — the MCRN has spent
forty years optimizing for Earth-as-enemy, while the UNN has
spent the same period optimizing for *not provoking Mars*.

## The Mars problem

The UNN's senior leadership has been quietly aware since
roughly 2335 that the MCRN, in a forced engagement, would win.
This is not a comfortable knowledge to hold. Public posture
maintains parity. Internal discussions at flag rank are
markedly more cautious. Earth's political class largely does
not know this; the few who do (the FSec director, the
Secretary-General, the chairs of the relevant assembly
committees) treat it as classified well above Tier-3.

The political consequence: the UN's foreign policy toward Mars
has shifted, since 2335, from negotiation-from-strength to
negotiation-from-equivalence. Most Earthers do not notice.
Most MCR officials do.

## The OSA and the *Halyard*

The *Halyard* is a private deep-range science vessel under
Mao-Kwikowski charter. Its registration is Belt-flagged. The
relevant UNN authority for the *Halyard*'s outbound leg is the
**Trans-Neptunian Trailing Point** OSA detachment — the cruiser
**UNS Iquique** and its sensor-beacon network.

Captain Marsh's outer-auxiliary career means she has personal
relationships with several officers in the Trailing Point
detachment. They are not her friends, exactly, but they
recognize her name. This will come up in play.

The *Halyard* will pass within OSA hailing range twice on the
outbound and twice on the return. Each hail is routine —
manifest check, life-support audit, courtesy. Most outbound
crews find these reassuring. A few find them claustrophobic.

## Notes for play

- A character with UNN service in their backstory is
  unremarkable; the OSA is the largest single employer of
  outer-system specialists.
- A character with Special Operations service is *highly*
  remarkable and should be discussed with the Keeper at
  Session 0. Possible. Carries narrative weight.
- The UNN is not the campaign's antagonist. They are mostly
  routine background. The Special Operations wing is a
  contingent threat the Keeper holds in reserve.
- ARTEMIS and SIGMA-4 will discuss the UNN openly. They will
  not discuss the Mars-parity intelligence (FSec Tier-3
  classified). They are not aware of it.
