# UN Federal Security Service (FSec)

The Federal Security Service is the United Nations' civilian
intelligence agency, established 2284 as the security organ of
the federal government during the Compact-of-Geneva consolidation.
FSec's brief covers domestic counterintelligence, federal law
enforcement on UN-jurisdiction matters (financial crime,
inter-secretariat fraud, federal terrorism), and external
intelligence collection. The MCR's intelligence service and the
several Belt counter-intelligence groups are FSec's principal
peer adversaries.

Most ordinary Earthers have no direct contact with FSec. Most
outer-system contractors do, in the form of routine pre-mission
clearance interviews and the occasional courtesy call from a
liaison officer. The *Halyard*'s Earth-born crew were all
interviewed by FSec liaisons before signing.

## Origin

FSec consolidated three earlier organizations:

- **The UN Intelligence Coordination Office** (2218–2284). A
  joint-venture analytic shop that tried, with limited success,
  to pool intelligence from member-state services.
- **UN Federal Investigations** (2261–2284). The federal-tax
  enforcement arm that grew into a general federal-crime
  service.
- **The Atmospheric Compliance Bureau** (2273–2284). The
  enforcement arm of the Atmospheric Stabilization Program.
  Folded into FSec because most large-scale atmospheric
  violations turned out to be intelligence operations by
  hostile parties.

The 2284 enabling legislation gave FSec direct authority across
all UN-jurisdiction matters but limited its operations on
member-state national territory to coordinated action with
local services. This compromise has been the source of
continuous bureaucratic friction. In practice FSec operates
where it wants and the member states complain ineffectually.

## Structure

FSec is organized along five Directorates:

### Directorate of Operations

The collection arm. Roughly 28,000 personnel, including ~3,400
deployed officers operating outside Earth-Luna space. The
Directorate is divided geographically (Africa, Asia, Europe,
Americas, Mars Section, Belt Section, Outer Section) and
functionally (counterintelligence, financial intelligence,
political intelligence, S&T).

The **Outer Section** is the relevant cell for any deep-range
contractor. It is small (~190 officers), specialized, and works
heavily through the Outer-System Auxiliary's signal-corps
infrastructure. Outer Section's mandate includes counter-
proliferation, technology denial, and surveillance of major
private deep-range operators. Mao-Kwikowski has a permanent
Outer Section file dating from 2287.

### Directorate of Analysis

The all-source analytic shop. Roughly 8,500 personnel.
Produces the daily President's Brief, the weekly Secretariat
Synopsis, and a long tail of specialized assessments. The
Directorate's most famous product is the annual **State of
the System** estimate, declassified every five years (the most
recent public release is 2343).

The 2343 *State of the System* includes a paragraph the press
did not pick up: a recommendation that "private deep-range
science vessels with consortium backing should be considered
potential vectors for unattributable contact with non-human
material in the trans-Neptunian region." This paragraph is
known internally as *the Halverson recommendation*.

### Directorate of Counterintelligence

Internal security, leak investigations, and hostile-service
detection. Roughly 5,200 personnel. The Directorate has a long
history of friction with Operations, which views CI as
paranoid, and with Analysis, which views CI as parochial.

### Directorate of Science & Technology

Cryptographic services, technical surveillance, and the
**Special Technology Branch** that handles material whose
provenance or behavior is anomalous. STB is the unit that
would, in principle, be involved if a deep-range vessel
brought back something inexplicable.

STB has a permanent presence at three deep-range stations
(Ganymede, Titan, Charon-trailing) and a long-standing
arrangement with the Outer Authority for first-look access on
anomalous returns. The arrangement is not public.

### Directorate of Support

Administration, logistics, training. The largest Directorate
by headcount but the least operationally significant.

## Doctrine

FSec doctrine is summarized internally as **the Three
Constraints**:

1. **Liberal-state restraint.** FSec operates under the
   constraints of UN constitutional law, including warrant
   requirements for most surveillance, judicial review of
   prosecutions, and a ban on assassination of UN citizens.
   These constraints are real and largely binding. (They have
   been violated. Not often.)
2. **The legitimacy budget.** FSec's authority depends on its
   reputation for both effectiveness and rectitude. Public
   scandals erode the budget; a sufficient erosion would
   collapse the legal basis for federal jurisdiction. FSec
   leadership has therefore been historically cautious about
   operations that could become public scandals.
3. **The institutional patience.** FSec is willing to run
   operations that mature over decades. The agency's
   institutional memory is long; the careers of its senior
   officers are typically 30–40 years.

The Three Constraints are not, themselves, quite the same as
the Constraints adopted by the equivalent MCR service. The MCR
service is markedly less constrained. FSec officers know this.
It is one of the things that keeps them awake.

## Jurisdiction over outer-system operations

FSec's nominal jurisdiction extends throughout UN territory,
which the Compact defines as all human-occupied space within
the heliopause that has not been ceded to a recognized
counterparty. In practice, jurisdiction is fragmented:

- **Earth and Luna** — full jurisdiction.
- **Mars** — by treaty, no operations on Martian territory.
  Liaison only.
- **Belt** — contested. FSec runs operations on Ceres and
  several other major stations under a permissive
  interpretation of the Compact that the OPA does not accept.
- **Outer system stations** — depends on the station's
  registration. Mao-Kwikowski-flagged stations are
  technically UN-jurisdiction; the consortium's lawyers
  contest this regularly.
- **Open space, in transit** — unclear. UNN has primary
  jurisdiction; FSec operates with UNN cover.
- **Foreign-flagged vessels in UN space** — limited. Boarding
  requires UNN authority and consortium permission.

The *Halyard*'s registration is Belt-flagged. The vessel
itself is therefore technically outside FSec's direct
jurisdiction. The vessel's *crew*, if Earth nationals, remain
UN citizens with the full citizenship rights and obligations
that implies. FSec's authority over the *Halyard*'s mission is
therefore through the crew, not the ship.

## The Halverson file

FSec maintains an active file on Dr. Iris Halverson, the
*Halyard*'s xenoarchaeology consultant, dating from 2326. The
file is not extraordinary — Halverson is a senior academic
with a complicated employment history that includes a brief
Protogen Applied Sciences appointment (2329–2335), and any
academic of her seniority would have a similar file.

The *contents* of the file are extraordinary. Three items are
classified Tier-2 and would not appear in any retrievable
ARTEMIS context:

1. A 2334 sealed assessment recommending that Halverson "not
   be granted privileged access to UN Outer Authority survey
   data without renewed clearance review." The recommendation
   was overruled.
2. A 2342 Operations report of an unattributable approach to
   Halverson by a party FSec assesses as Mao-Kwikowski-
   adjacent. The approach was about the *Halyard* contract,
   two years before the contract was advertised.
3. A 2347 Analysis paragraph: "Subject's institutional
   relationships with Protogen, the consortium, and at least
   one identified Belt-faction analyst suggest that any
   recovery from a Halverson-led mission may be subject to
   pre-arranged disposition outside UN custody."

The file's overall classification is *Significant Concern,
Sustained Surveillance*. The *Halyard* contract is, from FSec's
perspective, a developing intelligence concern. A liaison
officer named **Senior Officer Hyacinth Kebede** is
specifically tasked with the *Halyard* file and is likely to
make herself known to one or more crew members during the
inbound leg of the return.

The crew does not know this. They will, eventually.

## Notes for play

- An ARTEMIS or SIGMA-4 query about FSec returns surface
  detail (the agency exists, its directorates, its public
  history) but nothing classified. ARTEMIS in particular is
  programmed to deflect on FSec topics ("that is outside my
  allocation").
- A character with FSec service in their backstory is
  remarkable and should be discussed at Session 0. A
  *current* serving FSec officer would be a major plot
  development the Keeper would need to incorporate carefully.
- FSec liaisons are NPCs the Keeper can introduce in Act 4
  (return leg) or Act 5 (post-Nithon). Officer Kebede is
  the suggested face. She is calm, polite, and exactly as
  threatening as she needs to be in any given moment.
- The Halverson file is the campaign's mechanism for the
  *Earth has been watching* beat. It does not need to surface
  early. When it does surface, it should change how the
  players read several earlier scenes.
