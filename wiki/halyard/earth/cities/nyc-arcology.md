# New York Arcology Cluster

![The Cordon sea-wall at sunset; Lower Manhattan and the arcology platforms above](https://halyard.atlas-sjsu.duckdns.org/art/earth-nyc-cordon.png)

The New York Arcology Cluster — what most Earthers still
call simply *New York* — is the largest city in the US-
Canadian Confederation and the financial capital of the
Americas. The metropolitan area's 28 million residents are
distributed across the original five-borough core, the
Long Island and New Jersey extensions, and the cluster's
distinctive *aerial annex* — the elevated arcology platforms
that were built during the 2240s-2280s sea-level-defense
era and have become, in their own right, recognizable
districts of the city.

The contemporary New York is *layered*: the pre-twentieth-
century Manhattan grid persists at street level; the early-
twenty-first-century skyline is preserved as architectural
heritage; the 2240s-2300s arcology cluster rises above; the
sea-defense ring forms the city's working perimeter.

## Site

New York's site has been substantially modified by the
2240s-2280s sea-level-defense program. The Hudson and East
River entrances are protected by a major sea-wall complex
(the *Cordon*). Lower Manhattan, which would otherwise
have been progressively flooded since the 2150s, sits
behind the Cordon and below the sea-rise level — its
waterfront is *below the surrounding ocean*, a circumstance
the city's drainage and pumping infrastructure manages
continuously.

Most outer-borough coastal areas have been similarly
defended. The result is that New York retains its
twenty-first-century street pattern almost in full,
behind a continuously maintained engineering perimeter.

## Layout

### Manhattan

The original island, with its preserved grid. The Battery,
Wall Street, the Financial District (still the city's
financial heart). Greenwich Village, SoHo, the East
Village (now mostly residential heritage zones). Midtown
(the original twentieth-century skyscraper district,
preserved). The Upper East Side and Upper West Side
(residential heritage). Harlem (cultural heritage and
working-class residential). The original Manhattan retains
roughly 4 million residents.

### The Arcology Cluster

The contemporary residential and commercial expansion. Most
of the cluster is over the East River and Hudson River
(the Cordon enabled the construction of arcology platforms
*above* the water surface, supported on the Cordon's
structural framework). The cluster includes:

- **The East Stack** — six arcologies (1.6-2.4 km tall)
  stretching from Lower Manhattan to Brooklyn
- **The Hudson Stack** — four arcologies along the Hudson
  River side, anchored at Lower Manhattan and rising
  toward the West Side
- **The Bronx-Westchester Stack** — three arcologies in
  the northern reaches, lower density but substantial
- **The Queens Stack** — five arcologies eastward,
  anchored at the original LaGuardia airport site (the
  airport itself was repurposed)
- **The Brooklyn Stack** — four arcologies through
  central Brooklyn

Total arcology-cluster population: ~16 million.

### The Outer Boroughs and the New Jersey/Long Island
Extensions

The remaining ~8 million residents in the older outer-
borough neighborhoods, the New Jersey suburbs (now
substantially redeveloped), and the Long Island extension.

## The Cordon

The Cordon — the sea-wall complex protecting the city — is
itself a public space. The structure's top deck has been
developed as a continuous park-and-promenade encircling
the city. The *Cordon Walk* is the longest publicly-
accessible elevated walkway on Earth (62 km from the
Battery around through Brooklyn, Queens, the Bronx, and
back via the Hudson).

The Cordon is also the structural foundation for the
arcology platforms — without the Cordon, the over-water
arcology construction would not have been possible.

## Politics

New York City is the largest city in the Confederation
but is not the federal capital (Washington holds that
role) or the secondary capital (Ottawa). The city government
is led by Mayor Marcus Kim-Sokoloff, a former financial
regulator. The city's politics is *centrist-pragmatic*,
with substantial influence from the financial sector and
the city's universities.

National-level politics in the Confederation has the city
as one of several major political centers (alongside
Toronto, Chicago, Los Angeles, Washington, Atlanta). New
York's particular weight is in the financial and cultural
spheres rather than the political.

## Economy

New York's economy is roughly $5.4T UN-equivalent — by far
the largest single-city economy in the Americas. Sectors:

- **Finance** — the New York Stock Exchange and NASDAQ
  remain the largest equity markets on Earth. The city's
  banking, asset-management, and insurance industries
  are continental leaders.
- **Higher education** — Columbia, NYU, CUNY, the
  Rockefeller University, Fordham, plus dozens of
  smaller institutions
- **Cultural production** — Broadway, the publishing
  industry, the journalism establishment, the major
  museums, the contemporary art world. New York's
  cultural production is *traditional* in the sense
  that it inherits and continues the twentieth-century
  patterns; it is not the most innovative on Earth, but
  it is the most prestigious.
- **Healthcare** — the New York medical complex (Mount
  Sinai, Presbyterian, Sloan Kettering, NYU Medical) is
  one of Earth's largest concentrations of advanced
  medical institutions
- **Trade and shipping** — the port (now substantially
  the Newark and Elizabeth marine terminals) handles a
  large fraction of Atlantic freight

## Cultural texture

New York's cultural register has changed less than most
twenty-third-century cities:

- **The continuity register.** New York's cultural life
  has retained more of its twentieth-century character
  than any other major Earth city. The walking-pace
  street life, the bagel-and-deli food culture, the
  Broadway theater tradition, the publishing-and-
  journalism establishment — all continue with
  recognizable continuity.
- **The intensity.** New York remains, by general
  agreement, the *most intense* city on Earth — fast
  pace, dense interaction, high career-and-cultural
  pressure. Earthers from less intense cities find it
  exhausting.
- **The cosmopolitan internationalism.** The city is
  Earth's second-most internationally diverse (after
  London by some metrics, after Singapore by others).
  Most Earth cuisines are represented at high quality;
  most Earth languages are spoken by sizable
  communities.
- **The intellectual register.** The university and
  publishing infrastructure produces a continuous
  stream of public intellectuals, journalists, and
  critics. The New York Review of Books, the Times,
  the Atlantic-affiliated publications continue to
  set agendas in Anglophone Earth public discourse.
- **The aesthetic confidence.** New York's cultural
  production is *unembarrassed* in a way that the
  contemporary European register has lost. The city
  asserts taste; takes its assertions seriously.

## The deep districts of Manhattan

Like Shanghai's Pudong, parts of central Manhattan have
been built over to the point of losing direct sunlight.
About 8 km² of Lower Manhattan and Midtown are *deep
district* — engineered-daylight street level, with the
arcology platforms above blocking the sky. The deep
districts retain their pre-twentieth-century street
patterns; the experience is uncanny — historical
streetscape, completely artificial environmental
conditions.

## New York and the *Halyard*

No member of the *Halyard*'s standing crew is New York-born.
The city is a likely Earth-side passing-through point for
any Confederation-American PC. The Confederation's outer-
system contractor pipeline runs heavily through New York's
financial-and-academic networks.

The Manhattan-affiliated Newark Liberty Spaceport runs four
daily Earth-to-Luna shuttles and one weekly direct-to-Mars
long-hauler.

## Notes for play

- A New York PC will have a recognizable register —
  fast-paced, intense, intellectually confident.
- The financial-or-academic-or-cultural backstory paths
  are the most obvious for a New York PC.
- The deep-district background is, as in Shanghai, a
  useful texture for cosmic-horror fiction.
