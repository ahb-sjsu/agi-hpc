# Shanghai

Shanghai is the largest city in East Asia, the principal port
of the Yangtze delta, and one of the three cities (with
Lagos and São Paulo) that contend for the title of *largest
on Earth*. Its 58 million residents inhabit one of the most
extraordinarily layered urban environments in human space:
a city built up from a twentieth-century commercial port
through the Pudong era of the early twenty-first century into
the contemporary Pudong-North arcology cluster and the
Hongqiao expansion, with each generation of construction
preserved beneath, alongside, and inside the next.

## Site

Shanghai sits at the mouth of the Yangtze on the East China
Sea coast. The site has been heavily modified across two
centuries of climate-defense and reclamation work:

- The original Pudong waterfront has been hardened against
  storm surge with a massive sea-wall complex completed
  2247
- The original Puxi (west of the Huangpu river) has been
  largely preserved as a heritage zone with strict height
  limits, despite its prime location
- The Pudong-North arcology cluster — the city's primary
  residential and commercial area — was built 2241-2298
  on reclaimed land north of the original Pudong
- The Hongqiao expansion to the west has, since 2310,
  added another major residential cluster
- The Yangshan offshore island has been developed as a
  major freight and logistics hub
- The atmospheric-engineering systems above the city are
  among the most sophisticated on Earth; Shanghai's
  rainfall control is, for most of the year, total

## Layout

The contemporary city has six broad districts:

### Old Puxi

The preserved twentieth-and-twenty-first-century city west
of the Huangpu. Height-limited, walking-friendly,
intensively touristed. The Bund, the former French
Concession, the older universities (Fudan, Tongji, Jiao
Tong), the major museums, the older shopping districts.
This is what most off-Earth visitors picture when they
imagine Shanghai.

### Pudong (original)

The first-generation modern Shanghai, built primarily 1990s-
2030s. Now substantially preserved as architectural
heritage, with the iconic skyline of the original era still
intact. The Shanghai Tower, the Jinmao Tower, the Oriental
Pearl have become to Shanghai what the Eiffel Tower is to
Paris.

### Pudong-North

The contemporary Shanghai. The arcology cluster — six major
arcologies, each 1.4 to 2.2 km tall, plus dozens of smaller
arcology-class residential buildings. Most of the city's
22 million Pudong-area residents live here.

### Hongqiao

The newer western extension. Less dense than Pudong-North,
more residentially varied. The major Earth-Luna passenger
terminal (Hongqiao Spaceport) is here.

### Yangshan

The offshore freight hub. Some 8 million workers and family
on a series of artificial-island platforms. Most of
Shanghai's bulk-cargo handling occurs here.

### The Lower Yangtze Corridor

The riparian district stretching upriver, blending into the
greater Shanghai metropolitan area with a continuous
population gradient toward Suzhou, Hangzhou, and the rest
of the Yangtze delta megalopolis.

## Politics

Shanghai is China's commercial capital and the center of the
Lower Yangtze megalopolis. The city's political relationship
with Beijing has been *cooperative* throughout the
post-Compact era; Shanghai's mayors are typically rotated
through a national career path that includes major federal
appointments.

The current Mayor is Wang Mingxue, a former finance
ministry official. The city's politics are *technocratic-
centrist*, with strong emphasis on continued infrastructure
investment, climate-resilience programs, and the management
of the Lower Yangtze water system.

## Economy

Shanghai's economy is roughly $4.8T UN-equivalent — by
itself, larger than most national economies on Earth.
Sectors:

- **Finance** — Shanghai's stock exchange and commodities
  markets are the largest in Asia, second on Earth only
  to the New York Confederation markets
- **Manufacturing** — heavy industry continues at scale in
  the metropolitan area; lighter manufacturing has
  shifted west to Hongqiao
- **Logistics and shipping** — Yangshan handles enormous
  cargo volumes
- **Higher education** — Fudan, Tongji, Jiao Tong, plus
  newer institutions; combined enrollment ~1.4M
- **Cultural production** — Shanghai's animation
  industry, film studios, and gaming-industry cluster are
  Asia's largest
- **Pharmaceuticals and biotech** — secondary to Mumbai
  but substantial; the Lower Yangtze cluster is the
  continent's principal pharma-manufacturing center

## Cultural texture

Shanghai's cultural life has its own register, distinct from
Beijing's (more political, more traditional) and from
Guangzhou's (more commercial, more southern):

- **Cosmopolitan internationalism.** The city has been
  internationally oriented for two centuries. Shanghai's
  expat community is the largest in any Chinese city; the
  food scene has dozens of national cuisines at high
  quality; the bookshop and arts scenes are continuously
  international.
- **The long Chinese cultural memory.** Shanghai's
  museums, libraries, and universities preserve the
  longest continuous Chinese cultural tradition. The
  Shanghai Library's pre-modern Chinese collection is
  the second-largest after Beijing's.
- **The post-Compact register.** Shanghai's cultural
  production has emphasized *quietude*, *contemplation*,
  *attention to the unfolding present*. The city's
  contemporary literature and cinema are markedly
  different from the more politically engaged early-
  twenty-first-century Chinese cultural production.
- **Atmospheric layering.** Shanghai's atmospheric
  engineering is so total that the city has produced
  generations of residents who have not stood in
  uncontrolled weather. The cultural consequences are
  strange — a population that is *outdoor-comfortable*
  but only in environments humans have made.

## The deep districts

Shanghai's most distinctive feature, by some accounts, is
its *deep districts* — areas of the original twentieth-and-
twenty-first-century construction that have been built over
to such a degree that they no longer have direct sunlight at
street level. About 40 km² of the original Pudong is in
this state, and the trend has been continuing.

The deep-district streets are lit by spectrum-corrected
panels that match natural daylight precisely. Most residents
of the deep districts have never noticed they don't have
direct sunlight. Older Shanghainese, who remember the
pre-vertical era, sometimes find the deep districts
disquieting; younger residents find them simply normal.

The deep districts have produced a specific cultural
attitude: *we live in a built world*. Shanghai-deep
residents have, by widespread off-Earth observation, a
particular philosophical comfort with the proposition that
their environment is constructed all the way down. This is
not anxiety. It is, for many of them, reassurance.

## Shanghai and the *Halyard*

No member of the *Halyard*'s standing crew is Shanghai-born.
The city is, however, a likely passing-through point for a
PC backstory — the Lower Yangtze corridor is a major
contractor-talent pipeline for outer-system work, and any
PC with East Asian background may have spent time in
Shanghai.

The Hongqiao Spaceport runs four daily Earth-to-Luna
shuttles and one weekly direct-to-Ceres long-hauler.

## Notes for play

- A Shanghai PC's specific district background matters. *Old
  Puxi heritage*, *Pudong-arcology*, *Hongqiao
  middle-class*, *deep-district unusual* are different
  textures.
- Shanghai's cultural register — internationally oriented,
  cosmopolitan, post-Compact contemplative — makes
  Shanghai PCs natural fits for science, scholarship, and
  cultural-production roles.
- The deep-district background is a particularly useful
  texture for cosmic-horror fiction. A character who has
  grown up in an environment they understand to be
  entirely artificial has a specific philosophical
  resilience that other characters lack.
