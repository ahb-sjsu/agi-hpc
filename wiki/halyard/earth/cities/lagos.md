# Lagos

![Marina Stack arcology cluster, Lagos waterfront at dusk](https://halyard.atlas-sjsu.duckdns.org/art/earth-lagos-marina-stack.png)

Lagos in 2348 is the largest city on Earth and, by most
cultural and political measures, its capital. The Greater Lagos
Metropolitan Authority covers 4,800 square kilometers along the
Bight of Benin and houses 64 million people; the cultural reach
is larger.

## Shape

The city is layered and amphibious. Three distinct zones:

- **The Upland** — the inland portion, on the higher coastal
  shelf. Conventional vertical-district construction with the
  full atmospheric-engineering treatment. The most populous
  zone (38 million). The Marina Stack, Lagos's signature
  arcology cluster, defines the Upland skyline.
- **The Lagoon** — the central zone, built across the original
  lagoon and surrounding lowlands. A combination of raised
  causeway districts, floating residential platforms, and the
  oldest of the arcology installations. Twenty-two million
  people. The most architecturally distinctive part of the
  city.
- **The Coastal Defense Zone** — the seaward perimeter, hardened
  against storm surge and slow sea-level rise. Industrial and
  commercial; the major freight ports, the Lagos Beam-Power
  Station, the original Lagos Spaceport (now a museum, the
  current Lagos-to-Luna passenger terminal is at Tarkwa).

## Marina Stack

Marina Stack is a six-tower arcology complex begun in 2249 and
completed in 2278. It was the first arcology designed from the
ground up as an integrated atmospheric-engineering system —
heat-rejection masts, distributed water harvesting, micro-
climate control floor by floor. The towers stand at 1.2 km,
1.4 km, 1.4 km, 1.6 km, 1.8 km, and 2.1 km. The tallest tower,
**Stack Six**, holds the African Secretariat's Western Office
and a number of major corporations.

The stack is connected at three sky-bridge levels (the 88th,
156th, and 240th floors). The 156th-floor concourse is one of
Earth's most photographed urban interiors — a kilometer-long
public walkway with views in two directions, lined with
restaurants, bookshops, and the small temple of the
Atmospheric Engineers' Guild.

A residential apartment in Marina Stack runs from 4,000 UN
credits per month at the lower floors to 28,000 at the
penthouse tier. Most of Lagos's Strata live in Stack Six
penthouses or in the older waterfront blocks of Ikoyi.

## The cultural pull

Lagos is the cultural capital of Earth in a way that would
have surprised twenty-first-century observers. The current
generation of Earth's most-watched holo-serials is shot in
Lagos studios, in a hybrid English-Yoruba-Igbo creole that has
become the lingua franca of African popular culture. Lagos
fashion, Lagos music, Lagos cooking, Lagos literature — all
are dominant within Africa and increasingly so beyond.

Several specific institutions matter:

- **The University of Lagos**, the largest university on Earth,
  with 480,000 students. The Faculty of Speculative Studies
  (a hybrid of philosophy, history, and political theory)
  produces most of the public intellectuals of the African
  Secretariat.
- **The Lagos Conservatory of Music**, on Victoria Island. The
  conservatory's 280-year archive includes the largest single
  collection of West African pre-electronic music recordings.
- **The Pan-African Library**, opened in 2294. A continental
  archive that has the legal right to demand a copy of any
  publication in Africa. The library's reading rooms are open
  24 hours and free.
- **The Stack Six Concert Hall**, an acoustically remarkable
  performance space at the 240th-floor level of Marina Stack
  Six.

## Daily life in Lagos

A Working Provisioned Lagosian lives in a vertical district —
typically Ojo, Surulere, Ikorodu, or one of the lagoon
platforms — in a 35–55 square meter unit, takes mid-level
transit to wherever their contract work is, and spends evenings
in the ground-level streets that the city is famous for. The
Lagos street culture is loud, multilingual, and stretches into
the small hours; a city of 64 million does not sleep.

Food is served at all hours and from nearly every cuisine the
diaspora has accumulated. The signature street meal is
**suya** (skewered, spiced grilled meat) eaten with sliced
onion and tomato; the signature evening drink is **palm wine**
(now mostly fermented in industrial vats but still served from
clay vessels in many bars).

Religion: Christianity and Islam are both substantial, with a
significant secular minority and the small but visible
**Ifá Revival** that has been growing since the 2310s. The
city is religiously plural by design and tolerant by long
practice; sectarian violence has been historically rare.

## Politics

Lagos is the political center of the African Secretariat, the
seat of the Pan-Continental Assembly's African Caucus, and the
home of the Federal Office of Outer-System Affairs. National
politics (Nigeria as a constitutional state) intersect with
continental politics (the Secretariat) in a complex way that
locals navigate without naming.

The current African Secretariat (2342–2354 term) is led by
**Folami Onaolapo**, a former development economist whose
program has emphasized the integration of the African
Secretariat into the broader UN federal structure. She is
considered cautiously pro-UN, pro-Compact, and skeptical of
the Provision-Plus left.

## Lagos and the *Halyard*

Lagos is one of Earth's six passenger-rocket terminals (Tarkwa
Spaceport, the new one). A character departing Earth for the
outer-system contract chain typically goes through Lagos or
Wenchang. Tarkwa runs four daily Earth-to-Luna shuttles and
two weekly direct-to-Ceres long-haulers.

Two members of the *Halyard*'s standing crew have Lagos
biographies — Captain Marsh did her UNN officer training at
the West African Naval Academy on Lagos Island, and Halverson
holds an honorary appointment at the University of Lagos's
Faculty of Speculative Studies.

The Lagos street-food at Tarkwa departure terminal is reportedly
better than anything you will eat for the next eighteen months.

## Notes for play

- A character from Lagos is presumed to be from one of the
  vertical districts, working-class to comfortable. The
  Strata are a small minority and a Lagos-Strata character
  should be discussed at backstory time.
- Lagos bilingualism is the norm. Most characters from Lagos
  speak English and Yoruba (or Igbo, or Hausa, depending on
  family origin) at native fluency, plus one of the
  African-trade languages.
- The cultural confidence is real and worth playing as
  texture. A Lagosian character does not feel provincial in
  Ceres station.
