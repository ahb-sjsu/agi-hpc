# São Paulo

São Paulo is the largest city in the Americas, the third-
largest on Earth (after Lagos and Shanghai), the economic
capital of Brazil and Latin America, and one of the
continent's most architecturally distinctive metropolitan
environments. The city's 74 million residents inhabit a
sprawling urban landscape that, in the southern hemisphere,
plays the role that Lagos plays in Africa and Shanghai
plays in East Asia: *the* major metropolis of its region.

## Site

São Paulo is built on the Atlantic plateau of southeastern
Brazil, about 70 km inland from the coast at Santos, at an
elevation of roughly 760 m. The site's slight elevation has
been a post-climate-shift advantage; the city's climate is
generally moderate, the storm exposure is limited, and the
local atmospheric engineering has been less aggressive than
in coastal megacities.

The Tietê and Pinheiros rivers, which were heavily
polluted in the twenty-first century, have been
substantially restored since the 2240s. The river-corridor
parks are now a major recreational asset for the city.

## Layout

The contemporary metropolitan area covers roughly 8,000
square kilometers and forms a continuous urbanization with
several smaller surrounding cities (Santos, Campinas,
Sorocaba) merging into the greater São Paulo agglomeration.

Major districts:

### Avenida Paulista corridor

The central commercial-cultural corridor, preserved from
the early twenty-first century with continuous
redevelopment. The major museums (MASP, the Pinacoteca),
the cultural centers, the older corporate headquarters.
Walking-friendly, intensively used.

### Centro Histórico

The historic center, preserved as heritage zone with
strict height limits. The Praça da Sé, the older
churches, the colonial-era street pattern.

### Vertical São Paulo

The contemporary arcology cluster, primarily in the
Faria Lima and Vila Olímpia districts. About 16 major
arcologies plus extensive arcology-class residential.
Most of the city's contemporary commercial and
residential growth is here.

### Zona Norte and Zona Leste

The dense residential zones to the north and east of
center. About 22 million residents combined; mostly
mid-rise vertical districts, working-class to
middle-class.

### Zona Sul

The southern residential zones, including the older
established middle-class and upper-middle-class
neighborhoods. Substantial parkland (the Parque
Ibirapuera, the Cantareira State Park boundary).

### The Corridor Cities

The continuous urbanization toward Campinas (12M), Santos
(8M), and the smaller surrounding cities forms a single
megalopolis of approximately 110 million across the
greater corridor.

## Politics

São Paulo is the political and economic center of Brazil
and Latin America. The city government is led by Prefeito
(Mayor) Henrique Costa-Mendoza, a former economist. The
city's politics are *centrist-progressive* by
contemporary Latin American standards.

Brazil's federal government is in Brasília, but São Paulo's
economic weight gives it disproportionate political
influence. Most major Brazilian federal initiatives are
shaped, in some way, by São Paulo's political and
business establishment.

## Economy

São Paulo's economy is roughly $4.4T UN-equivalent. Sectors:

- **Finance** — São Paulo's stock exchange (B3) is the
  largest in Latin America and the Southern Hemisphere.
- **Manufacturing** — substantial heavy and light
  manufacturing across the corridor.
- **Cultural production** — Brazilian cinema, holo-
  serials, music, and literary publishing dominate
  Latin American cultural markets and have substantial
  global reach.
- **Higher education** — USP (Universidade de São
  Paulo) is the largest university in Latin America;
  Mackenzie, FGV, USP-affiliated institutions, dozens
  of others.
- **Services** — the largest services economy in Latin
  America.

## Cultural texture

São Paulo's cultural register is distinctive:

- **The bandeirante register.** Brazilian cultural
  identity has a strong *bandeirante* (frontier, expansion,
  entrepreneurial) component, with São Paulo as the
  historical center. The contemporary city retains a
  recognizably entrepreneurial energy that is unusual on
  Earth.
- **The cultural confidence.** São Paulo is unembarrassed
  about being the cultural capital of South America. The
  city's literary, musical, and visual-art production is
  prolific; its artists are taken seriously across
  Earth.
- **The food culture.** Brazilian cuisine — heavily
  evolved from Portuguese, indigenous, African, Italian,
  Japanese, and Lebanese influences — is one of the
  Americas' great fusion traditions. São Paulo's
  restaurant scene is varied, prolific, and
  internationally respected.
- **The football substrate.** Football remains, in São
  Paulo as in most of Brazil, the dominant cultural
  reference. Three major clubs (Corinthians, Palmeiras,
  São Paulo FC) anchor the city's sport-cultural life.
- **The night life.** São Paulo's nightlife is, by
  widespread acknowledgment, the most vital in Latin
  America. The city's bars, restaurants, music venues,
  and clubs operate at a scale and quality that other
  Latin American cities admire.

## São Paulo and the *Halyard*

No member of the *Halyard*'s standing crew is São Paulo-born.
The city is, however, a likely Earth-side passing-through
point for any Latin American PC. The Latin American
contractor pipeline for outer-system work runs heavily
through São Paulo's corporate-and-academic networks.

The city's airport (Guarulhos International) handles the
largest passenger volume in the Americas. Earth-Luna
shuttles run from the affiliated Spaceport SP twice daily;
the Earth-Mars passenger service makes São Paulo a major
intercontinental hub.

## Notes for play

- A São Paulo PC's specific district background is less
  important than for Mumbai or Shanghai; the city's
  cultural register is more uniform across districts.
- The Brazilian-Portuguese language is a useful PC
  asset; many Mars-born have Portuguese-Brazilian
  background.
- The *bandeirante* energy is a useful character
  texture. São Paulo PCs default to entrepreneurial,
  forward-leaning, and slightly confrontational.
