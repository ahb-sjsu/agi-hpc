# Europe and Anatolia in 2348

Europe-Anatolia is the *cultural museum of humanity*.
Demographically depopulated relative to its twenty-first-
century peak, economically supported by tourism and
remittances, politically less load-bearing than its
international diplomatic prominence suggests, the continent
is — by widespread off-world description — *pleasant,
aging, and slightly melancholy*. Europeans don't disagree.

The region's 1.1 billion residents live in a continent that
has been, for most of the past 150 years, in the slow process
of becoming what it had always partially been: a place that
preserves and transmits culture rather than producing
contemporary economic and demographic mass.

## Demographic outline

Europe-Anatolia's 1.1 billion is distributed roughly:

- **Western Europe** — 380 million. France, the Iberian
  Peninsula, the Low Countries, the British Isles, Italy,
  the German-speaking lands. The most depopulated and
  most preserved of the European zones.
- **Central and Eastern Europe** — 290 million. Poland,
  the Czech Republic, the Balkans, Hungary, Romania, the
  Baltic states, Ukraine, Belarus. Mixed; some areas have
  been more stable demographically than Western Europe,
  others less.
- **Russia (European portion)** — 180 million. Moscow and
  St. Petersburg dominate; the Russian Far East is
  administratively counted with Asia for Secretariat
  purposes.
- **Anatolia** — 140 million. Turkey and the Caucasus
  states. Politically grouped with Europe but
  demographically and economically more vigorous than
  most of European proper.
- **Scandinavia** — 110 million. The Nordic states,
  including Iceland and Greenland (Greenland is, in 2348,
  a populated landmass with its own constitutional
  status; the climate shift opened the southern coast for
  agriculture).

The continent's median age is 58 — by far the oldest of
Earth's continents. Demographic decline began in the late
twentieth century in Western Europe, spread eastward through
the twenty-first, and stabilized at low replacement rates by
the 2230s.

## The depopulation and its consequences

Europe's depopulation is, to outsiders, the continent's
defining feature. Most European cities have lower
populations in 2348 than they had in 2025. London is
roughly 8 million (vs. 9 million in 2025). Paris is
roughly 6 million (vs. 11 million). Madrid is roughly
3 million (vs. 6 million). Most rural areas have lost more
than half their twenty-first-century populations.

The consequences:

- **Beautiful, half-empty cities.** The major European
  capitals retained or rebuilt their pre-twentieth-century
  cores. Paris, Vienna, Prague, Florence look much like
  they did in 1880, with maintenance and conservation
  rather than continued construction. The streetscape is
  pedestrian-friendly, the architecture is intact, the
  museums are well-funded. There is also less *life* on
  the streets than in any comparable Asian or African
  city.
- **Tourism as economic backbone.** About 28% of Europe's
  GDP is tourism. The continent receives roughly 800
  million tourists annually, mostly from other Earth
  regions but with substantial off-world numbers. Many
  Belters and Martians visit Europe specifically as a
  *cultural pilgrimage*.
- **The remittance economy.** A substantial fraction of
  the European population draws income from descendants
  who emigrated to Africa, Asia, or off-world. The
  remittance flows are large enough to be a major
  category in the continent's national accounts.
- **The institutional preservation.** European
  universities, museums, archives, and cultural
  institutions have been preserved aggressively. The
  Sorbonne, Oxford, Cambridge, Bologna, Heidelberg, the
  Hermitage, the Louvre, the Vatican Library, the
  British Museum all retain or have rebuilt their
  collections and continue to operate at roughly their
  twenty-first-century scale despite serving smaller
  local populations.

## Political form

The European Secretariat administers UN programs across the
region under the rotation system. The current Secretary is
Marielle Vasquez-Bender, a Spanish-Portuguese former
constitutional lawyer.

Below the Secretariat, the old national governments persist.
The European Union, which was a major political institution
through the early twenty-first century, was largely
absorbed into the UN federal structure during the
2240s-2270s consolidation. Some EU institutional
machinery survives as cultural and educational
infrastructure (the Erasmus program, the European Court of
Human Rights, several research-funding bodies) but the EU
qua state is, in 2348, a historical period rather than a
present polity.

National-level politics in Europe is generally quiet. The
United Kingdom is constitutional-monarchic; France is
republican; Germany is federal-republican; Russia is
constitutional with a strong elected presidency. Most of
the smaller states have similar formal constitutions with
similar quiet politics. Political parties exist; elections
happen; not much changes.

The most politically *active* European region is Anatolia.
Turkey's politics remained vigorous through the
twenty-second and twenty-third centuries; the Anatolian
political class has the highest rate of UN federal
positions per capita of any European region.

## Major cities

### London (8M)

The largest English-speaking city in Europe. The British
financial sector remains globally significant; the
publishing industry is a continental leader; the
universities (Imperial, UCL, the Russell Group beyond,
plus Oxford and Cambridge nearby) are among the most
prestigious in human space. London's atmospheric
engineering has been kept relatively conservative; the
city retains a recognizable pre-twentieth-century street
pattern in much of its core.

### Paris (6M)

The cultural capital of Europe. The Louvre, the Centre
Pompidou, the Sorbonne, the Bibliothèque Nationale all
operate at full scale. The city's restaurant culture is
the most prestigious on Earth (yes, even compared to
Lagos and Tokyo; the prestige is partly inherited and
partly maintained). The Seine has been dredged and
restored; the city has substantial green infrastructure.

### Berlin (5M)

The intellectual and political capital of contemporary
Germany. The Humboldt University and Free University are
major educational institutions; the city's reputation as
a cultural laboratory dates from the late twentieth
century and has been continuously sustained. Berlin is
the European city most likely to surprise non-Europeans
with its contemporary energy.

### Moscow (12M)

The largest European city by some accounting (some
classifications put it as Asian; the Secretariat
geography lists it as European). Russia's political,
financial, and cultural center. The Kremlin, the
Bolshoi, the State Hermitage's Moscow annex, Moscow
State University. The city has retained a more vigorous
demographic trajectory than most of European proper,
partly because of inflows from the Russian Far East.

### Istanbul (16M)

The largest city in Europe-Anatolia. Politically counted
as European since the 2243 administrative decision;
culturally and historically Anatolian. The Bosphorus
remains one of the most strategically and culturally
significant waterways on Earth. The Hagia Sophia (a museum
since the late twenty-first century) is the most-visited
religious site on Earth that is not currently a place
of worship.

### Rome (3M)

The Vatican is here. The Catholic Church's institutional
center. The city is, to most Catholic Earthers, a
pilgrimage destination at least once in a lifetime. The
Roman antiquities — the Colosseum, the Forum, the
Pantheon — are the most-visited classical-era ruins on
Earth.

### Reykjavik (0.4M)

The capital of Iceland. The Lunar Industrial Belt's
northernmost Earth-side communications relay is here.
Politically and culturally distinct; in 2348 a haven for
philosophical retreat, with several major contemplative
communities and an unusually high proportion of Earth's
working-class intellectual community in residence.

## Culture

European culture in 2348 is *preserved* and *late*. The
preservation is excellent; the lateness is real.

Specific markers:

- **The classics curriculum.** European universities still
  teach Latin and Greek in their classics programs, ancient
  philosophy in their philosophy departments, and pre-1900
  literature as the core of their literary studies. This
  is a Europe-specific commitment; African, Asian, and
  American universities have moved past it.
- **The high-art aesthetic.** European art-making is
  primarily *interpretive* — the production of new
  responses to the existing canon rather than the
  generation of unprecedented work. There are exceptions
  (the Berlin scene, Reykjavik's experimental
  contemplative literature, the Anatolian pop-electronic
  movement). The dominant register is curatorial.
- **The slow life.** Most European public life moves at
  a markedly slower pace than the rest of Earth. Cafés,
  parks, libraries, bookshops, museums, walking tours
  are the dominant social institutions in most European
  cities.
- **The melancholy register.** European literature,
  music, and cinema have been substantially attentive to
  the *late, beautiful, fading* register for the past
  century. This has been criticized as self-indulgent. It
  is also, by widespread off-Earth assessment, *exactly
  correct* — Europe has been late and beautiful and
  fading, and its cultural production has named the
  experience honestly.

## The continent's posture

- **Toward Earth-other** — gracious, diplomatic, slightly
  remote. European political culture has the most
  practiced internationalist register of any region but
  also the smallest demographic and economic weight.
- **Toward Mars** — formal. European-Martian relations
  are diplomatically correct. Mars's republican
  vigor is, to most Europeans, slightly exhausting.
- **Toward the Belt** — sympathetic-distant. Belters
  visit Europe; Europeans visit the Belt rarely.
- **Toward Luna** — popular. Many older Europeans retire
  to Luna; Lunar-European cultural exchange is
  substantial.

## Notes for play

- A European PC will have a different texture than
  Africans, Asians, or Americans. The post-Compact
  European register is *cultivated*, *educated*, *aware
  of being from a small place*, and slightly defensive
  about it.
- Europe is the *easy* off-world stop for an Earther
  who wants to begin acclimating. A European PC who has
  lived in the Belt or on Mars for a few years will have
  noticeably *transcended* the European register, which
  is partly why they took the contract.
- The cultural pilgrimage from off-world is a useful
  narrative resource. A *Halyard* PC might have a
  European cultural moment in their backstory — a year
  in Vienna studying music, a summer at the Sorbonne,
  a winter walking the Camino. These are unremarkable
  for sophisticated 2348 Earthers.
