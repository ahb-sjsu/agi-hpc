# Australasia and Oceania in 2348

The smallest of Earth's major regions by population — 0.7
billion residents across an expanse larger than any other
continent — Australasia-Oceania has spent the past century
quietly defining itself as a place that does things slightly
differently from the rest of Earth. The continental
twenty-first-century crises (Pacific sea-level rise, the
Great Barrier Reef collapse, the Millennium Drought) forced
adaptations that the region has come to consider its
distinguishing achievement.

The result is a region that is *small*, *innovative*, and
*surprisingly unchanged in cultural register* relative to its
twenty-first-century counterparts. Australasian Earthers find
this both gratifying and slightly suspect.

## Demographic outline

The 0.7 billion residents are distributed:

- **Australia** — 220 million. Mostly along the eastern,
  southern, and southwestern coastal corridors. The
  interior remains sparsely populated; the climate-shift
  brought intermittent flooding rather than the
  catastrophic drying some twenty-first-century models
  predicted.
- **New Zealand** — 80 million. The North and South Islands
  carry the population. New Zealand's growth has been
  steady; the country has been one of the most consistent
  recipients of climate-displaced populations from other
  Pacific regions.
- **Papua New Guinea and the Melanesian states** — 130
  million.
- **The Philippines (administratively counted with Asia
  but culturally pan-Pacific)** — covered in `earth/
  regions/asia.md` for political purposes.
- **The Polynesian states** — 90 million across Hawaii,
  Samoa, Tonga, French Polynesia, the Cook Islands, the
  smaller polities. The major demographic story is the
  rise of *floating-platform habitats* off Hawaii and
  French Polynesia.
- **The floating-platform habitats** — 180 million across
  approximately 24 large platforms in the Pacific. These
  are the region's most distinctive innovation.

## The floating-platform habitats

The defining contribution of Australasia-Oceania to twenty-
third-century urbanism. Built primarily 2230s-2310s in
response to the dual pressures of:

- Sea-level rise displacing low-lying Pacific island
  populations
- Climate-stress driving Pacific Rim populations to seek
  alternative living arrangements

The habitats are large floating structures — typically 8 to
20 km in their longest dimension — that operate as
self-contained communities of 4 to 16 million people each.
Construction is modular; ballasted floating modules linked
to form a continuous urban surface, with sub-surface
infrastructure (water, sewage, transit, energy) and
above-surface structures (residential, commercial, public).

Major platforms:

- **Hawaii Cluster** — three large platforms anchored
  west of Hawai'i Island, with combined population ~24
  million. Politically and culturally part of the
  expanded Hawaiian Republic.
- **French Polynesia Cluster** — five platforms in the
  Tahiti vicinity, ~28 million.
- **Tonga-Samoa Combined** — two platforms, ~16 million.
- **Bismarck Sea Cluster** — four platforms off PNG,
  ~32 million. The most industrialized of the platform
  clusters.
- **Tasman Sea Platforms** — three platforms between
  Australia and New Zealand, ~24 million. The most
  recently built.
- **Smaller platforms** — about 7 additional platforms
  in various locations, totaling ~56 million.

The platforms are an Oceania political achievement. They
required the displacement-and-relocation of populations
across what had been national boundaries; they required
multi-decade collaborative engineering across small
states; they required substantial UN-Earth coordination.
The platforms have stabilized a population that would
otherwise have been forced into mass migration to other
continents.

The platforms also have a distinctive culture. Platform-born
residents identify as *Platformers* or *Sea-born* depending
on local convention; their culture mixes Pacific
traditions with the engineering-pragmatic register of the
construction era; the food cultures incorporate
extensive sea-resource use that mainland populations have
adopted only partially.

## Australia

Australia's twenty-third-century settlement pattern is
substantially coastal. The interior, while not as desert-
catastrophic as twenty-first-century projections feared, is
sparsely populated outside the major mining centers
(Mount Isa, Kalgoorlie, the Pilbara). The population
concentrates along:

- The east coast (Sydney 12M, Brisbane 8M, Melbourne 9M,
  the smaller coastal cities)
- The south coast (Adelaide 4M, the smaller coastal
  cities)
- The southwest (Perth 6M)

Australia's economy is diversified between mining
(continued at substantial scale, particularly the rare-
earths and fusion-grade fuels), agriculture (recovered
substantially since the Millennium Drought ended), and
services. The country is a major exporter of higher
education and biotechnology; its universities (Sydney,
Melbourne, ANU, UWA) are the continent's principal
research institutions.

Politically Australia is a constitutional monarchy
(retaining the British Crown nominally, though the
constitutional relationship has been substantially
attenuated since the 2240s) with a federal democratic
government. Australian politics is *quiet* in the European
sense, but with more vigor than European national
politics; the country retains a recognizable Australian
political register that twentieth-century observers
would recognize.

## New Zealand

New Zealand has been one of the major receiving
countries for Pacific climate-migration. The country's
population has nearly tripled since 2080. The
infrastructure has been substantially expanded; Auckland
is now 18M, Wellington 6M, Christchurch 5M, with
continuous coastal urbanization between.

The country retains its distinctive cultural register —
egalitarian-pragmatic, environmentally conscious,
politically vigorous — and has integrated the migrant
populations relatively successfully. Māori cultural
authority has been continuously respected and increasingly
prominent in national institutional life.

New Zealand's economy is diverse: agriculture
(particularly dairy and the recovered wine industry),
services, biotechnology, and a substantial space-sector
component (the Mahia Peninsula remains a major Earth-Luna
launch site, with newer infrastructure having grown up
since the 2210s).

## Polynesia, Melanesia, Micronesia

The traditional Pacific island cultures have largely
maintained continuity through the climate transition,
though many of them are now substantially platform-based.
Hawaii, Samoa, Tonga, French Polynesia, the Cook Islands,
the Solomons, Vanuatu, Fiji, Kiribati, the Marshall
Islands, the Federated States of Micronesia, Palau —
each retains a distinct political and cultural identity,
some on traditional landmass, some on platforms, most a
mix.

The cultural production of the Pacific in 2348 is rich.
Music, dance, literature, and visual art have all
benefited from the platform era's prosperity and the
inter-island connectivity that the platform infrastructure
enables. The Honolulu-Apia-Auckland triangle has become a
recognized continental cultural circuit.

## Politics

The Australasia-Oceania Secretariat administers UN
programs across the region. The current Secretary is
Hone Te Rangi-Lopez, a Māori-Polynesian former
infrastructure engineer. The Secretariat's politics
emphasize:

- Continued platform expansion and maintenance
- Pacific climate-resilience programs
- The complex relationships with Asia and the Americas
  Secretariats over Pacific shipping and trade

Australia retains the largest national-level political
weight in the Secretariat; New Zealand is second; the
Pacific island states form an important coordinated
caucus.

## Culture

Australasia-Oceania's cultural register is distinct:

- **Pragmatic environmentalism.** A strong cultural
  commitment to environmental sustainability that is
  more *pragmatic* than the European or African
  variants. Done because it works rather than because
  it expresses values.
- **Egalitarian informal social register.** Australian
  and New Zealand social culture has retained the
  *first-name informality* that twenty-first-century
  observers identified as characteristic. This has
  spread to most of the platforms.
- **Sea-and-sky aesthetics.** Pacific cultural
  production has always been attentive to the sea and
  the sky; the platform era has reinforced this. The
  region's literature, music, and visual art are
  unusually focused on water, weather, and the
  horizon.
- **Sport.** The Australasia-Oceania region has, per
  capita, the highest sports participation rate on
  Earth. The Pacific Games are one of the major Earth
  athletic events; cricket, rugby, surfing,
  Australian-rules football, and several Pacific-
  traditional games all retain large followings.

## The continent's posture

- **Toward Earth-other** — collegial, pragmatic,
  occasionally feeling small. The Secretariat is the
  smallest Earth-side voice, but it has been
  consistently effective at advocating for its
  interests.
- **Toward Mars** — friendly. Australasian-Martian
  cultural exchange is substantial; many Mars-born
  have Australasia or New Zealand family connections.
- **Toward the Belt** — strong. The Pacific shipping
  industry has long-standing ties with Belt commerce;
  the platform-construction expertise has been
  exchanged with Belt habitat-building efforts.
- **Toward Luna** — moderate. The New Zealand-Luna
  passenger route is well-established; substantial
  cultural exchange between Auckland and Tycho.

## Notes for play

- A Pacific platform-born PC is one of the more
  distinctive Earth-PC backgrounds available. The
  cultural register (pragmatic, environmentally
  conscious, egalitarian) is recognizably distinct.
- An Australasian PC is, statistically, more likely
  than other Earth-PCs to have spent significant time
  off-world. The region's space-sector engagement is
  substantial.
- The *quiet competence* of the regional cultural
  register makes Australasian-Oceania PCs natural
  fits for engineering, environmental science, and
  contract-management roles.
